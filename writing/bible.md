# Story Bible — Survive the 70s

> The rewriting target for every AI-drafted line. If a line doesn't match
> what's below, rewrite it.

## Premise
Saturday, July 14, 1979. The narrator is a **14-year-old boy** in a
generic American suburb (vibes loosely modeled on Baltimore County, MD —
split levels, station wagons, a creek behind the houses, woods that
everyone just calls "the woods," a 7-Eleven a 10-minute bike ride away,
and at least one older brother in the neighborhood with a Camaro).

Mom has issued the standard directive: "Go outside. Be home when the
streetlights come on." Five scenes, one day, one ending.

**Why 14:** old enough to bike across town unsupervised, joyride
somebody's older brother's car, try genuinely dumb things (m-80s, sips of
warm Schlitz, the first hit off a joint that immediately makes him
question everything), have a crush, get caught, and navigate older kids
who treat him like a mascot. The fun is in *getting the stuff*, not just
having it.

## Narrator voice
- **Adult-you in 2026 looking back**, not a kid in real time.
- Dry, affectionate, mildly horrified. Think David Sedaris doing
  voiceover on a home movie. *Freaks and Geeks* with sharper teeth.
- Past tense, first person. ("I lit the m-80. This was, of course, a
  small bomb.")
- The comedy comes from the gap between *what the 14-year-old thinks is
  happening* (he is being smooth, brave, cool) and *what the adult
  narrator now understands was happening* (he was a moron in Toughskins
  jeans and a Members Only knockoff).
- **The narrator never says the slurs.** That's a key tonal line — he
  winces at them, comments on them, sometimes openly. "Somebody at the
  table called him a retard, because in 1979 that was a thing you could
  just say and nobody flinched."

## Inner voices (max 3 — keep it simple)
1. **Common Sense** — quiet, usually right, usually ignored. Sounds a
   little like your dad.
2. **Dumb Kid Bravery** — loud, charismatic, the reason we're here.
   Sounds like the kid in the back of the bus who already broke his arm
   once this year.
3. **Mom (Imagined)** — the voice in your head reciting things Mom has
   already said this week. Triggered by especially bad ideas.

## Tone & Edge

**This is not a politically correct game.** It's 1979. Kids talked like
kids in 1979. Sanitizing it makes it not funny — and not true.

### What's IN
- **Period slang including the ugly ones.** Retard, spaz, queer, fag,
  dyke, gay (as generic insult), dweeb, dork, douche, dipstick. Used
  **in character, peer-to-peer**, the way 14-year-old boys actually used
  them — as cheap insults thrown at friends who missed a jump, not as
  hate aimed at real groups.
- **Smoking, drinking, weed, joyriding.** The fun is in *getting it* —
  finding a guy outside the liquor store, scoring a roach off an older
  kid, sneaking the keys to the Pinto while the older kids are in
  the pool. The chase is the comedy.
- **Crushes, dates, getting shot down.** Karen at the 7-Eleven, the
  older girl who maybe smiled at you, working up the nerve to call a
  girl on a rotary phone with her dad answering.
- **Consequences.** Hangovers at church, Dad finding the empty, the cop
  who knows your dad, getting grounded, walking the bike home with the
  chain off, puking in the bushes, the principal's office Monday.

### What's OUT (the bright lines)
- **The N-word.** Period accurate, but the game isn't about that. It
  crosses from edgy comedy into a different kind of writing. Not in.
- **Slurs in the narrator's voice.** Narrator is adult-you in 2026, he
  knows better. The kids in the scene say them; the narrator notes them.
- **Hate as the joke.** A character whose entire bit is racism, no.
  That's lazy and it isn't funny.
- **Anything sexual involving the 14-year-old.** Crushes, awkward
  flirting, getting rejected, fine. On-screen sex, no.
- **Drugs/booze with zero payback.** If every joint is awesome and
  every joyride ends in triumph, the comedy goes flat. *Something*
  goes wrong every time — that's the rule.

### How to use the ugly slang well
- **Aimed at peers, not real targets.** Somebody missed the creek jump?
  "You retard." Stranger on the corner? Never.
- **Density: sprinkle, don't dump.** A scene with two of those words
  feels like 1979. A scene with eight feels like the writer has a
  problem.
- **Bullies say it more. Crushes say it less.** The cool girl has
  better material.
- **The narrator's wince does the heavy lifting.** "He used a word I
  will not repeat here, and we all laughed, because we were 14 and
  awful." That line is funnier and edgier than the slur itself.

## Tonal Rules
- **Punch up, or punch sideways at past-self.** Joke is on the era, on
  the dumb 14-year-old, on the parents' generation. Never on real
  marginalized groups in the actual present.
- **No nostalgia worship.** The 70s were dangerous, dumb, and weird.
  The humor depends on telling the truth about that, not romanticizing
  it.
- **No real brand names.** Every real-world brand, band, movie, product
  gets a fake parody equivalent. See `world.md` for the canonical
  parody list. F is for Family style — sidesteps copyright AND makes
  the comedy sharper, because every parody name is itself a joke.
- **Cursing:** R-rated but not gratuitous. Shit, ass, damn freely. Fuck
  sparingly, for emphasis when a 14yo actually would.
- **Stakes are real but cartoon.** You can break a bone, get grounded
  for a month, lose your bike privileges. You can't actually die.
  Failures are funny, not grim.

## Era detail toolbox (sprinkle, don't dump)

**Physical hazards:**
lawn darts (Jarts), metal slides, BB guns, ten-speeds with sketchy
brakes, the way-back of a station wagon, garden hose water, Pop Rocks +
cola panic, secondhand smoke fog, Stranger Danger vans, homemade
Halloween candy, asbestos popcorn ceilings, leaded gasoline fumes,
trampolines with no nets, m-80s, bottle rockets aimed at friends, the
creek "you can totally jump."

**Social hazards:**
older kids behind the 7-Eleven, the older brother with the Camaro,
the buy-us-beer guy outside the liquor store, the abandoned house at
the end of the street, the mean lady who calls the cops if you cut
across her yard, the gym teacher who *was* in the war and never quite
came back, the priest, the principal.

**Substances:**
warm Schlitz from somebody's dad's garage fridge, a single can of
Budweiser passed between four kids, peach schnapps from a Mom's purse,
Boone's Farm (somebody's older sister's), a roach an older kid left in
the ashtray, an entire joint somebody scored from a guy at the mall,
Marlboro Reds (older kids), Kools (cousin), Tareytons (the burnouts).

**Objects:**
rotary phone with the long curly cord, CB radio, 8-track, transistor
radio with a single earpiece, mood ring, sea monkeys, Stretch
Armstrong, Schwinn Varsity or generic ten-speed, slip 'n slide,
Easy-Bake Oven, walkie-talkies that don't work past 30 feet, the
Sears catalog, Slurpee, baseball cards in bike spokes, a Members Only
jacket somebody's older cousin has, roller skates with a key, Atari
2600 (just arrived, the rich kid has one), a black-and-white portable
TV with rabbit ears, dad's Polaroid.

**Cars on the block:**
Pinto (everyone makes the explosion joke), Gremlin, Pacer ("the
fishbowl"), Vega (rust), Nova, Chevelle, Camaro (older brother), VW
Bus (the hippies), wood-paneled station wagon (everyone's mom), AMC
Eagle. The keys are usually in the visor.

**Sounds:**
screen door slap, ice cream truck (sounds creepy in hindsight),
cicadas, the clatter of plastic on a tube TV, AM radio (WBAL in
Baltimore for vibes), the click of a Bic lighter, a Big Wheel on
concrete, a rotary phone dialing, a Polaroid spitting out a photo.

**Smells (mentioned, not simulated):**
chlorine, hot vinyl car seats, lawn mower exhaust, Coppertone,
secondhand smoke, the funk of a basement bedroom, weed (described
through 14yo POV: "skunk that had been on fire"), beer breath.

**Phrases Mom uses:**
- "Be home when the streetlights come on."
- "Don't make me come out there."
- "We have food at home."
- "Wait 30 minutes after eating or you'll cramp up and drown."
- "Close the door, were you raised in a barn?"
- "I'll give you something to cry about."
- "Wait till your father gets home."

**Phrases the kids use:**
- "Bitchin'"
- "Rad"
- "Far out" (older kids still say it, ironic by '79)
- "Psyche!" (when you fake somebody out)
- "What a trip"
- "Bogus"
- "Burnout" (a person — pothead older kid in a Sabbath t-shirt)
- "Narc" (anybody who might tell)
- "You're so [retarded/gay/queer/spaz]" — peer insults, see rules above

## Skill Check writing pattern
Every check has THREE outcomes written:
1. **Crit success** — the kid genuinely pulls it off; narrator marvels.
2. **Pass** — works, but barely.
3. **Fail** — something goes wrong; usually the funniest line.

Fail should rarely be a dead end. It costs a stat or opens a dumber path.

## Forbidden (one more time, clearly)
- N-word.
- Anything sexual involving the 14-year-old protagonist.
- Detailed real violence (a BB welt or busted lip is fine; gore is not).
- Slurs in the narrator's voice.
- Real political figures or events as direct punchlines.
- Modern internet voice ("bestie," "no cap," "rizz"). Narrator is in
  2026 but doesn't talk like Twitter.

## Tagline candidates (for the title screen)
- *Be home when the streetlights come on.*
- *We didn't have helmets. We had each other. We had bad ideas.*
- *Five scenes. One Saturday. Don't tell Mom.*
