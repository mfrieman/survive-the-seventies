## SEED 1 — The Evel Knievel Stunt Cycle Distraction (1973)

**The kernel:**
The Evel Knievel Stunt Cycle (Ideal Toy Corp, 1973) was THE iconic 1970s wind-up toy — a friction-rev gyro-launcher with a poseable Evel figure that you cranked up with the red "Energizer" handle and let RIP across the floor / sidewalk / driveway. **Real, peaked 1975-76, perfect timing.**

**Mark's pitch:** Tie a Slim Jim to the back of the cycle, rev it, send it across the ground — **dogs chase the cycle**, Mark sneaks past while the guard dog is distracted.

**Mechanic hook:** CRAFT (combine Stunt Cycle + Slim Jim) → STEALTH (timed window to slip past).

**Period payoff:**
- The Stunt Cycle itself is a perfect "remember THIS?" / "WHAT IS THAT?" two-audience object.
- Slim Jim (1968 launch, peak 1970s, corner-store staple). Already a perfect period prop.
- The combination is the kind of dumb-elegant solution a 14yo would actually come up with. *Diegetic problem-solving.*

**Quest context candidates:**
- Breaking into someone's yard for a part (Pete's-equivalent basement? Old Man at end of block?)
- Junkyard run for go-kart wheels (junkyard dog is the *classic* obstacle)
- Sneaking into the back of the movie theater
- Stealing chrome caps off a parked semi (truck stop has a dog)

**Open:** Is the Stunt Cycle also a *part for the machine* (one of the 10 components — small gyro?) or just a quest tool we keep and re-use?

## SEED 2 — Phone Coin-Return Sweep (Free Money Mechanic)

**The kernel:**
1970s kids checked the coin return slot of every payphone they passed. People forgot change. Sometimes a quarter. Sometimes a dime. Sometimes nothing for a week. A real, learned, automatic habit.

**Mechanic hook:** FIND (passive, ambient). Add coin-return slot as an *examinable* on every payphone in every zone. Random low-value loot. Most days: nothing. Sometimes: a nickel. Rarely: a quarter. **Free in-world cash drip that rewards exploration and observation.**

**Period payoff:**
- Teaches that payphones EXISTED and were everywhere.
- Teaches the *texture* of poverty / scarcity in kid life — a quarter was a thing you'd notice.
- Mark VO first hit: *"Wait — there's MONEY in there sometimes? People just LEFT it?"*

**Compounds with SEED 3 (payphone calls).**

**Open:** Should we add a tiny "lucky day" probability bump tied to Tear/rep states for variety? Or keep it pure RNG with a daily cap?

## SEED 3 — Payphone Has Its Own Number (Mission Use)

**The kernel:**
Every 1970s payphone had its own number printed right on the unit. **Anyone could call that number and the payphone would ring.** Kids used this to communicate. It was a real thing. Also a hazard: someone else using "your" payphone when an expected call was incoming = a small social crisis.

**Mechanic hook:**
- DIAL-IN: Mark gives an NPC the number of a specific payphone. NPC is supposed to call at a specific time. **Mark must be at that payphone in that window.**
- DEFEND: while waiting, someone else (NPC) might walk up and use the phone. Mark has to *get rid of them* (talk, distract, intimidate, lie) before the call comes through.
- INTERCEPT: alternatively, Mark could *pick up someone else's incoming call* on a payphone and learn something.

**Period payoff:**
- Communication-without-cell-phones is one of the foreign-to-Gen-Z concepts the game has to sell. This mechanic makes it *load-bearing,* not just ambient.
- The Mark VO around standing at a payphone for 15 minutes hoping a person dials it is gold.

**Mission candidates:**
- Setting up a rendezvous with Danny (impossible — Danny's gone). Cut.
- Setting up a rendezvous with Iwona to meet at the boardwalk. **STRONG.**
- Coordinating a heist where a confederate calls Mark from a different location to confirm timing. ALSO STRONG.
- Tear-related: the notebook gives Mark a number to call. He calls it. ???? answers. (Save this for a major beat — Act 2 or 3.)

**Open:**
- Does the payphone-as-receiver mechanic happen multiple times (small) or once (big set-piece)?
- The "someone else using your payphone" defend-mechanic is *very* sitcom-funny. Confirm we want to build it as a real mini-mechanic.

## SEED 4 — Easy-Bake Pot Brownies

**The kernel:**
Use the Easy-Bake Oven (real, in `banned-toys.md`, 100W bulb at 350°F) to bake pot brownies. The juxtaposition of *children's toy* + *illicit substance* + *very-1976 problem-solving* is the whole comedy.

**Mechanic hook:** CRAFT (Easy-Bake + box mix + pot) → produces a tradable / consumable item.

**Period payoff:**
- The Easy-Bake is iconic.
- Pot in 1976 was in *peak* cultural-presence-but-still-illegal mode. The smell, the rolling paper, the disco-vinyl-cover-with-seeds-cleaned-on-it. Period.
- Box-mix brownies (Betty Crocker, Duncan Hines) were a 1970s suburban staple.

**Comedy beat:** Mark VO: *"I am 14. I am using a children's toy. I am making controlled substances. This is the most legitimately illegal thing I have ever done and it's not even the third weirdest thing today."*

**Trade target candidates:**

- A specific adult NPC? Risky — let's stay kid-on-kid.

**Open:**

- Source of pot: the dealer kid. Where does HE get it? Older brother? His uncle? We don't need to answer this — but we should know it has an answer.

## SEED 5 — Build a Go-Kart (Quest)

**The kernel:**
A real 1970s kid project. Mark dumped the parts list:
- **Lawn mower engine** (probably stolen from a neighbor's garage or scavenged from a busted mower at the dump)
- **Siphoned gasoline** (real method: garden hose, mouth, gravity, oh god)
- **Plywood platform** (scrap pile, lumber yard, construction site)
- **Wheels from a shopping cart** (heist: roll a cart out of a grocery store parking lot, pop the wheels at home)
- *(Implicit: rope steering, no brakes except feet, helmet??? no.)*

**Mechanic hook:** This is a **full multi-step quest** using all four acquisition mechanics:
- FIND: scrap plywood, busted-mower scavenge
- TRADE: bolts/screws from old man at hardware store
- EARN: convince an older kid (Mickey?) to actually help solder/weld
- CRAFT: assembly at the workbench

**Period payoff:**
- **Siphoning gas with your mouth** is one of the perfect Gen-X-remembers / Gen-Z-recoils moments. Mark VO: *"You don't just — you don't just SUCK on the hose until — oh god yes you do. Yes you DO."*
- Shopping cart wheels: lovingly stolen. The casual property-crime baseline of 70s kid culture.
- The completed go-kart is a **status-symbol-in-the-block.** Once you have one, kids respect you. Casual rep boost.

**Quest-result options:**
- "Mark builds a go-kart for a different kid in exchange for a needed part." (Mark's pitch)
- "Mark builds a go-kart for himself and uses it for movement / mini-game." (Bigger build.)
- **Best of both:** Mark builds one *for* a kid (Bobby? a younger kid?), but gets to use it during a specific quest before delivery.

**Risk hook:**
- Tear ripples slightly more when Mark builds something this complicated (machine-adjacent CRAFT).
- Possible "go-kart breaks at 25mph downhill" beat — the Jump scene's energy applied to wheels. Mark survives because *Danny survives.* But Mark genuinely thought he was going to die.

**Open:**
- Is this an Act 1 quest (early signal of capability) or an Act 2 quest (escalation)?
- Does the go-kart persist as a vehicle on the map after the quest? **Pitch: yes, it's parked in the yard. Adds to set dressing. Sometimes a kid asks to borrow it.**
- Engine NOISE is a *huge* part of this. Loud. Annoying neighbors. Possible rep cost in certain zones.

## SEED 6 — Radio Dedication to Iwona

**The kernel:**
A real 1970s thing: a local AM/FM DJ ran a once-weekly request show where kids called in and dedicated songs to crushes. The dedication played live on air. Other kids heard it. Some parents heard it. *The crush sometimes heard it.* Mid-tier social-blast device.

**Mechanic hook:** A single, deliberate **quest beat** rather than a system.
- Mark calls the station (use the payphone, dial the request line).
- Holds for an operator. Multi-step phone-tree mini-conversation.
- Picks a song from a short list (player choice — affects tone of the moment).
- Picks dedication wording (player choice — affects how Iwona reads it).
- Waits.
- Cuts to scene: the dedication airs on Mary Kowalski's kitchen radio while Iwona is washing dishes. Iwona's reaction shot.
- Cut to: Mary's reaction shot. (She heard it too. Knowing look. Maybe a small smile.)

**Period payoff:**
- Public romantic gesture in a no-internet world had *real social weight.*
- The kitchen radio scene is one of the most Gen-X-tear-jerking, Gen-Z-fascinated moments we can build.
- Mary catching it without Iwona knowing she caught it = pure family-comedy gold.

**Story payoff:**
- A strong mid-Act-2 romance beat. Possibly the moment Iwona's perception of Mark shifts from "interesting" to "this boy is *specifically about me.*"
- Sets up Mary as an unspoken ally — she *knows* and chooses to let it happen.

**Open:**
- Which fake song do we dedicate? Needs the **fake-brand-and-song naming pass.**
- Which DJ persona do we build? Pitch: **"Big Sal" or a similar nighttime AM DJ** — smoky voice, says "kid" a lot.
- Does Iwona *acknowledge* she heard it in their next scene? Pitch: **no.** She says nothing. She just looks at him a beat longer than usual. The player has to read it. Iwona's whole character is *understatement.*

## SEED 8 — "The Small Bus" / 70s Casual Cruelty Baseline

**The kernel:**
Mark's note (verbatim): *"There were the small yellow buses for idiot school kids and retards — Good for joking about someone need to take the small school bus tomorrow because they are stupid."*

**The historical reality:** Yes — short school buses were used for special-education transport in the 1970s, and casual slurs based on them ("riding the short bus," "r-word," etc.) were *common kid vernacular.* Period-accurate.

**The design problem:** This is one of the genuinely *tricky* tone calls. Mark has explicitly said the comedic lineage is **Carlin / Pryor / Cheech / Robin Williams / Married with Children** — pre-PC, push at lines, *smart* edge. He's also said *"smart edge, not shock for shock's sake."*

**My recommendation (pitch — for Mark to weigh in):**

We capture this as **period-accurate kid cruelty** WITHOUT putting the slur in the player's mouth.

What works:
- Mickey (the hard-edged kid) **uses** the language casually. The audience hears it. Mark VO **does not adopt it.** Mark — from 2026 — visibly recoils. *"They — they just SAID that. Out loud. About a kid. And the other kids LAUGHED. And nobody — Christ."*
- The joke is the *Mark-from-2026 reaction*, not the cruelty itself. This is exactly how *F is for Family* handles its hardest material.
- We use "short bus" as the device, not the worst slurs. It threads the needle — it's period-accurate, it's clearly cruel, it lets Mark react, and it doesn't make the game itself the cruelty vehicle.

What I would NOT do:
- Put the worst slurs in Mark's narration. Mark is our 2026 conscience. He can witness 1976. He shouldn't *be* 1976.
- Make this an extended bit. One scene, the cruelty lands, Mark reacts, we move on.

**Period payoff:**
- One of the harder *"what was it actually like"* truths. Important for the game's honesty.
- Two-audience: Gen X knows this was how kids talked (uncomfortably). Gen Z is genuinely floored.

**Open — needs Mark's call:**
- **Approve the "Mickey says it, Mark recoils" approach?** Or do we want Mark to participate, or do we want to skip this entirely?
- If approved: which specific scene gets it? Probably one of Mickey's introduction beats, not a romance scene.
