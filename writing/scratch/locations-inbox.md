### The Vet — character canon (locked 2026-05-24)

**Owns the store** (updated 2026-05-24). Not an employee. He came back from Vietnam and *built* this — turned the war's leftovers into a livelihood. That's a thousand times more dignified than "stuck behind a counter" and unlocks real authority: his call who buys the bayonet, his call to refuse a sale, his call to close early. The store IS him.

**Store name candidates:** "Sonny's Surplus" (parody-of-real, Mark's lived reference — Sonny's was the real Baltimore chain). Mark to decide whether to keep "Sonny's" as the parody name, or invent something. Leaning: **keep "Sonny's Surplus"** — it's authentically Baltimore, the kind of name a real vet would put on his shingle, and it doesn't carry IP risk the way a brand name would. Sonny is *not* the Vet's name — Sonny is the *store's* name (could be a dead brother, a dog, a callsign, or just "what felt right" — TBD as character develops).

**The Pocket Knife memory (Mark's real anecdote, 2026-05-24):** Mark spent *months* saving up for a pocket knife at the real Sonny's Surplus as a kid. This is the **emotional spine** of how the store should feel:
- A long-savings quest object is canon-worthy. Most quests in this game are days. ONE quest should be **weeks** of in-game time — the player commits to it, comes back to the store repeatedly, the Vet starts to recognize them, the savings progress is visible.
- This is *exactly* the pacing-equalizer the Tear system was already designed to enable. A long quest doesn't bore the player if it's interleaved with everything else.
- **Strong candidate:** the pocket knife is itself a CRAFT TOOL Mark needs for one of the machine components (stripping wire? cutting cork gaskets? scoring glass?). Promotes from "kid memory" to **functional spine** — and the months-of-savings feels earned because it has to.

Implications of ownership:
- The store is a **fixed point** in The Oaks' geography — like Burnsville, but with a *person* anchoring it. Mark can always find him there.
- **His discretion is sovereign, not policy.** When he reads a kid and refuses the sale, that's character, not bureaucracy.
- **Late-Act possibility:** when the Tear escalates and reality starts breaking near the machine, the surplus store is a candidate location for the Tear to *register* in a way only HE would notice. He sees the world the way a soldier does — anomalies stand out. That's the setup for the highest-stakes "do I tell him?" Mark Test, with a built-in justification for why he'd already half-know.

**NOT "half-crazy."** Avoid the cartoon-unhinged-vet trope — it's both lazy and historically painful for real vets, AND a half-crazy person couldn't run a business. The Vet that lands harder, both comedically and emotionally:

**Reference character: Burt Gummer (*Tremors* series).** Locked 2026-05-24.
- Competent, prepared, slightly intense, dryly funny
- Takes things seriously that others don't
- **Lucid and capable** — could absolutely own and operate a business
- Stockpiles for problems most people don't see coming, and is occasionally right
- Quietly affectionate with people he's decided to trust; impenetrable to everyone else

That IS the Vet. Same energy, applied to a 1976 Baltimore surplus shop.

- **Hyper-vigilant, dryly funny, slightly stuck in 1969, lonely.**
- **The smartest adult in Mark's life.** A man you'd trust with your life. Organizes his garage like a fire-team perimeter.
- **"Charlie's still out there"** — uses the slang for Viet Cong in conversation. Only HE uses it. Sparingly — ~3 times across the game:
  - **1st use:** lands funny-strange to the player (Mark VO: *"He thinks the Viet Cong are going to land at the 7-Eleven."*)
  - **2nd use:** recontextualizes — *oh, he means it.* Not paranoia in the cartoon sense. Vigilance that never got turned off.
  - **3rd use:** heartbreak beat. He's not joking; he never has been.
- **Bobby parrots "Charlie" once → Kay snaps "We don't say that."** This one beat is the writers' room saying *the era used the word, the game knows it's loaded.* Does the awareness-work for the player without the script lecturing. Era-accurate AND modernly-conscious.
- **Why he's dangerous to Mark's cover:** he's *lucid,* not cracked. He'd actually figure out the machine if Mark told him. That makes the story-listening minigame (the trust-build) the highest-stakes adult relationship in the game outside Iwona.
- **Hyper-vigilance comedic register:** the Mark Test moments here are about *what he sees* — he reads kids before he sells them anything. A 10-year-old gets a gas mask; a 17-year-old gets refused a bayonet. He's *right* about each one.
- **Comedic lineage check:** edge-walking from character truth (Carlin / Pryor / Married with Children locked tone), not shock-for-shock. The slur is voiced **by** a character the script clearly knows is using a dated word — the show is smarter than the character about the character's own words. (Disco Elysium / Mad Men / MWC move.)

**Open beats for later:** name, age (~28-35 in 1976 = served as a young enlisted), specific in-war event (one detail, never elaborated), what he reads when the store is slow, whether he has a dog at the store.
