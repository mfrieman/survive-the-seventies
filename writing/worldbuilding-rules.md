# Worldbuilding Rules — The Oaks, 1976

> **Status:** LIVING doc.
> **Purpose:** Capture the *structural* rules that distinguish 1976-from-2027 in this game. These are the rules that make the world *feel* like 1976 without us having to lecture the player about it. Each rule is a *system* the player encounters through gameplay, not a paragraph the player reads.

---

## Rule 1 — Every Store Has An Owner

**Locked 2026-05-24.**

Every commercial location in The Oaks is owned by an identifiable person who is **usually on premises**. The owner is also a *character* with opinions, regulars, history, and a stake — not just a vendor.

**Why this matters mechanically:**
- We don't have to invent 30 NPCs. **Stores ARE the NPCs.** Every recurring destination is anchored by a person Mark can build a relationship with.
- The owner has *authority.* They can refuse a sale, set rules, close early, remember a face. (See: the Vet's discretion at Sonny's Surplus.)
- Cover-test interactions ("you know how to use this?") work because the cashier is the *owner,* and owners read kids.

**Stores with owners locked or roled:**
| Store | Owner | Status |
|---|---|---|
| Burnsville Electronics | **Steve Burns** (son, current owner) + **Stan Burns** (father, founder, retired-but-can't-stop) | LOCKED 2026-05-24 — see Rule 10 |
| Sonny's Surplus | The Vet (Burt-Gummer-coded Vietnam vet) | character locked, name TBD |
| Record & Tape Traders | TBD | role only |
| Loch Ridge Hardware | TBD | role only |
| Loch Ridge Pharmacy | The Pharmacist | role only |
| Pizza & Sub Shop | TBD | role only |
| (others as quests pull them in) | | |

**Danny's canon line establishing the rule** (Act 1, early Book entry from Danny's side):

> *"I was at the place and asked to speak to the owner and they looked at me like I was retarded... The future sucks!!!"*

This line:
1. Establishes the "future sucks" theme in Danny's voice
2. Names a specific era-detail (independent ownership disappeared)
3. Foreshadows Danny's swap motivation (he didn't time-travel for *fun*)
4. Sets up a recurring engine: every store visit, Danny can drop a *"this place will be a CVS by 2003"* / *"this place will be a Starbucks and the espresso will cost ten dollars"* line in the Book
5. Uses "retarded" as period-accurate slang the script clearly knows is loaded — same handling as "Charlie" (see Rule 3 below). Use **1-2 times across the whole game,** never more.

## Rule 2 — Mark-from-2026 Recoils at Normalized Hazards

(Already locked in tone docs.) Every era-teaching beat lands by showing 2026-Mark recoil at something 1976 considered normal:
- Wood-burning kits sold to children
- Gas masks for $5
- Riding in the back of a pickup truck
- No seatbelts, no helmets, no safety chains on swing sets
- Lawn darts (literally Component C)

The script doesn't lecture. **Mark VO does the recoil; the world does the rest.**

---

## Rule 3 — Period-Loaded Language Is Used Sparingly And Knowingly

**Locked 2026-05-24.** Some 1976 vocabulary is loaded for modern players. Rather than scrub or wallow, the script uses it **sparingly** and **with awareness baked into the staging.**

| Word | Who uses it | How often | The script's "I know" beat |
|---|---|---|---|
| "Charlie" (for VC) | **Only the Vet.** ~3 times across the whole game. Funny → meant → heartbreak. | 3 max | Bobby parrots it → Kay snaps *"We don't say that."* |
| "Retarded" | Danny (in Book entries), 1-2 kid characters in passing | 1-2 max | TBD — possibly the Danny "future sucks" line is itself the awareness beat (it lands as a *Danny-voice* moment, which the 2026 player registers as "oh, the kid from the past says things differently") |
| Other 1970s slang | As needed | Sparingly | Always staged so the script-as-a-whole knows it's loaded |

**The principle:** *The script is smarter than its characters about its characters' own words.* (Mad Men / Disco Elysium / Married with Children move.) Period-accurate without endorsement.

We do NOT use racial or homophobic slurs. The era-language we DO use exists in a narrow band: vocabulary that's *dated and culturally awkward* rather than *targeted bigotry.* Where the line falls is a craft judgment, not a rule — when in doubt, leave it out.

## Rule 4 — Cover Is Currency

(Already locked in Mark Test design.) Every social interaction in 1976 has *witnesses* in a way 2026 doesn't. Strangers in your neighborhood **know you, notice you, remember you.** Anonymity is not the default. The cover is a resource the player spends, sometimes deliberately.

---

## Rule 5 — Doors Knock, Phones Ring, Adults Are Reachable

In 1976, the way you contact someone is to **physically go to where they are** or **call a landline that rings in their kitchen.** No texts, no DMs. Mark's plans require *logistics.* This isn't a gimmick — it's a mechanic. Several quests will route through the player physically traveling to ask a question that in 2026 would be one tap.

---

## Rule 6 — The Knowledge-of-Danny Tier System

**Locked 2026-05-24.** Every NPC who exists in Mark's world sits in one of five tiers based on **how well they knew Danny before the swap.** The tier dictates the social puzzle Mark has to solve to interact with that character.

| Tier | Definition | Cover required | Examples |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Tier 0** | Arrived **after** the swap. Never knew Danny. | **None.** Mark gets to be himself. The only relief in the game. | **Iwona + the Kowalskis** (moved to The Oaks after the swap). Possibly other Tier-0 arrivals during the game. |
| **Tier 1** | Strangers. Don't know Danny. | Light. Just be a kid. | Ocean Side strangers, distant store owners, passers-by |
| **Tier 2** | Acquaintances. Know Danny's face only. | Mild — "haven't seen you in a while" energy. Cover survives ordinary politeness. | Pharmacist, hardware store owner, gas-station guy |
| **Tier 3** | Regulars. Know Danny well, know his patterns, **have opinions about him.** | High — every interaction is a small social puzzle. They expect Mark to remember things, know things, be mid-conversation about projects. | **Burnsville Electronics owner** (prototype Tier 3 NPC), librarian, corner store guy |
| **Tier 4** | Intimates. Know Danny too well to fool. | Cover-as-performance fails. Story question: tell them? Or partial honesty? | **The Vet** (target of the trust arc), **Bobby** (would notice if he weren't 7 and routinely dismissed), **possibly Kay or Dennis** depending on how close they get |

**Why this matters for Iwona specifically:** Tier 0 is the romance's quiet superpower. Iwona is the only person on the block where Mark gets to be honest-ish — *not because she's earned trust* but because *there's nothing to lie about.* That's why falling in love with her is so easy and so terrible. Easy because no performance is needed. Terrible because she's the only relief, and he may have to leave her.

**Player-facing experience:** the player **never sees these tier labels.** They discover the tier of each character through interaction — the cost of "act natural" reveals itself as the puzzle.

## Rule 7 — Honest Humility Beats Performed Expertise (with Adults)

**Locked 2026-05-24.** In Tier 2 and especially Tier 3 conversations, the *right* play for Mark is almost always to **lean into his age** and ask honestly, not to fake competence.

In 1976:
- **Kids are allowed not to know.** Adults will explain to a 14-year-old without suspicion — that's the cultural script.
- A kid who *pretends* to know what he doesn't is *suspicious;* a kid who shrugs and asks is *normal.*
- Mark's 2026 brain assumes he has to perform expertise (because that's how 2026 social media trained him). 1976 *rewards* not performing.

**This is a teachable game pattern.** Early Burnsville visits teach the lesson; later high-stakes moments (the Vet, Iwona's father, Dennis when he gets too close) reward the player who learned it.

**Failure mode:** if the player tries to bluff with a Tier 3 NPC and gets called on it, the cover takes a small ding but doesn't break — the game gives them another chance to revert to humility. Lesson, not punishment.

## Rule 8 — The Box of Leftovers (Act 1's Forensic Engine)

**Locked 2026-05-24.** Danny built the original machine in his bedroom. The fire destroyed the **machine**, not the room. So when Mark wakes up, he inherits:

- ✅ The Book (Danny's notes, schematics, parts list)
- ✅ The workbench (intact)
- ✅ Danny's tools (soldering iron, hand tools — survived the fire)
- ✅ A **box of leftover parts** Danny had collected but not yet installed
- ✅ **Burnsville Electronics receipts** (LOCKED 2026-05-24) — Danny saved them, several of them, tucked into the box. Yellow carbon-copy "Burnsville Electronics / Loch Raven / Stan or Steve Burns, Prop." with hand-written part descriptions and dates spanning months. These receipts are the player's **physical onboarding cue** — "you've been going here, you have a relationship with these people, here's where to start." They also seed every later Burnsville interaction: Stan can pick up a part, glance at the stapled receipt, and *prove* he knows Danny ("that's my handwriting on the bag, kiddo"). Receipts > exposition.
- ✅ A list of ~20 parts needed for the machine (NOT the same as the 10 named spine components — see below)
- ❌ The machine itself (destroyed, charred remains)

**The numbers:**
- **~20 total parts** needed to rebuild (per Danny's plans)
- **~10 already covered** by what's in the box of leftovers
- **~10 still need to be acquired** during the game (these map to the 10 named spine components in `components/index.md`)
- Numbers can flex (7 final parts might play better than 10 — calibrate during prototype)

**Act distribution:** most acquisitions in Acts 1-2, with **one big capstone in Act 3 = Component A (Gilbert U-238 Atomic Energy Lab).**

**Why this matters mechanically:**
- Eliminates the plot hole of Mark needing to *buy* a soldering iron (Danny already had one)
- Creates a **forensic Act 1 opening:** Mark inventories the box, identifies parts, figures out what's missing
- Establishes the *real* Act 1 mechanic: **knowledge-extraction social puzzles** (see Rule 6, Tier 3)
- Some box items Mark can identify on sight (Slinky-tier — obvious); some he cannot (these become the "go ask a Tier 3 owner what this is" loop)
- Provides ongoing late-act reveals ("wait, *that* was what this thing was for?!")

**First Burnsville visit reframe:** the first trip is NOT to buy a soldering iron. It's to **identify mystery parts from the box** while operating Mark's cover with the Tier 3 owner who knew Danny. (See `component-L-soldering-iron.md` for the reframed scene.)

## Rule 9 — Pre-Internet Knowledge Acquisition (the Library Loop)

**Locked 2026-05-24.** Mark's 2026 brain wants to photo-search every mystery object and ask Google. He **cannot.** Instead, he can:

| Source | Trust cost | Cover cost | Speed |
|---|---|---|---|
| **The library** (card catalog, encyclopedia, asking the librarian) | None | Low — kids in libraries are normal | Slow (a half-day) |
| **Burnsville owner** | None — he likes Danny | Tier 3 cover risk if mishandled | Fast |
| **Sonny's / the Vet** | High — Vet sees Mark too clearly | Tier 4 cover risk; spend wisely | Fast |
| **Dennis** (Mark's dad) | Costs Dennis-attention currency; thin supply | Tier 4 risk if Dennis gets curious | Fast for construction-ish, slow for electronic |
| **A kid who might know** | None | Wastes time; kids rarely actually know | Slow + uncertain |

**Every "what is this?" is a small social transaction with a real cost.** That's the second gameplay engine — the first being the machine spine itself.

**Library = Zone 2 anchor location, opposite Burnsville.** Add to locations canon when locations.md is created.

## Rule 10 — The Burns Family (Burnsville Electronics canon)

**Locked 2026-05-24.** Burnsville Electronics is a two-generation family business. The dynamic is one of the warmest in the game and serves as a **structural model** for how a 1976 family business *could* work (contrast: Sonny's Surplus = one man alone).

### Stan Burns (the father, the Mentor) — ~65-70s

- **Founded Burnsville Electronics** as a young man (post-WWII or early '50s — TBD). Built it from nothing. Possible immigrant background (consistent with the surname being an anglicization — though "Burns" can also just be Burns; not locked).
- **Handed the business to his son Steve.** Steve runs it now and runs it well.
- Stan is **at the shop every day** (for now — his wife may eventually pull him into shorter hours; deferred). Works the floor. Talks to customers. **Teaches kids who come in with parts they don't understand.**
- **He doesn't need to be there to make money. He's there because being useful is what he was put on Earth to do.**
- The teaching is **his joy AND his quiet ache.** He's proud his son didn't need his help to run the shop; he's also a man whose life's work runs without him now.
- **Will not retire to Florida.** Has been asked. Has refused.
- **Tier 3** — knew Danny well, has opinions about him (positive, "good kid, builds things"), but did **not** know about the machine project. Cover risk is real but warm — Stan is on Mark's side.
- **Honest humility wins with Stan.** He LIGHTS UP when a kid says "I don't know what this is." That's why he's there.

### Steve Burns (the son, the Owner) — ~40s

- **Current owner.** Took over from Stan ~5-10 years ago. Excellent at it. Runs books, manages inventory, handles suppliers.
- **Loves his father.** Wants Stan to enjoy himself. **Does NOT secretly resent the free advice** — he's *proud* of it.
- Recurring beat: Steve teases Stan from the back counter while Stan explains resistors to a kid:
  > **Steve** (calling from the back): *"Pop! You gonna retire today or tomorrow?"*
  > **Stan** (without turning around): *"I'll retire when I'm damn ready."*
  > *(Both of them laughing.)*
- Steve knows his father doesn't want a Florida condo. **Wants his dad to die in the shop, happy, mid-explanation.** Doesn't say it that way. Just gives him the space.
- **Tier 2-to-3 floating** — knows Danny's face, knows the family name, doesn't have the deep bond Stan has with Danny. Less cover risk than Stan.

### Why this works for the game

1. **Stan is the third father.** Dennis Marek (sheetrocker, quiet) is Danny's biological dad. Mark's 2026 dad is offscreen. Stan is the one who *teaches*. The one with *time*. Mark didn't know he was looking for him.
2. **The Burns family is a counterweight to the Marek family.** Dennis-and-Kay are loving but worn-down. Stan-and-Steve are functioning, joyful, generational success in motion. The contrast does work without ever being underlined.
3. **The Stan/Steve banter is the game's warmest recurring beat.** Every Burnsville visit can carry one. Free comedy + free emotional grounding.
4. **Theme this delivers:** *Every grown child eventually surpasses their parent. The story shows the bittersweet beautiful side of that.* (Locked thematic move.)
5. **Era-teaching beat (LOCKED 2026-05-24):** When Mark sees the Stan/Steve dynamic — a son running the shop, his father on the floor teaching kids, both laughing about retirement — Mark's 2026 brain registers: *this isn't a franchise. This isn't a corporate location. This is a FAMILY who OWNS this place.* The lesson lands without a lecture. A short Mark VO after Steve's first interrupt does the work. This is the **first Rule 1 ("Every Store Has An Owner") teaching beat** the player experiences in actual gameplay — the abstract rule becomes a felt thing the moment Mark watches a father and son tease each other across a workbench. Couple with a future-sucks Book entry callback ("In 2027 every store is a CDN of itself") to seal it.
