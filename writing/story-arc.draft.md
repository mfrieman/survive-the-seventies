## NAMING CONVENTION (read this first)

**Always refer to the consciousness by name. Never the body.**

- **"Mark"** = always the future-kid (the player). Even when in Danny's 1976 body.
- **"Danny"** = always the 1976 host. Even when in Mark's 2026 body.

After the swap, **Mark is always in 1976** and **Danny is always in 2026.** The era tells you the body — no need to spell it out.

**Drop "Mark as Danny" / "Danny as Mark" entirely.** Just write "Mark" and "Danny."

| ❌ Old | ✅ New |
|---|---|
| "Mark as Danny walks downstairs" | "Mark walks downstairs" |
| "Mark as Danny tells Danny as Mark he hates it" | "Mark tells Danny he hates 1976" |
| "Danny as Mark in the future replies" | "Danny replies from 2026" |

**When we need to specify the body** (rare — only for what *other characters see*), use parentheticals:
- *"Kay sees Danny (Mark) walk in."* — Kay sees the 1976 body; Mark is inside.
- *"Mark catches his reflection — Danny's face stares back."*

**In dialogue, in-game characters use the body's name** (because that's who they think they're talking to):
- Kay says *"Danny, set the table"* → she's talking to Mark.
- A 2026 friend says *"Yo Mark"* → talking to Danny.

This honors **Thematic Pillar 1: Soul Not Body** — the name follows the soul.

## Cross-Cutting Anchors (locked threads — do not violate)

These are creative load-bearing decisions that the acts must honor:

- **PAL** — In 2026, Mark's only "voice" is an AI companion called PAL. Smooth, ever-attentive. Theme: humans only talk through machines now. Mark losing PAL when he wakes up in 1976 is a real psychological loss.
- **Parody 1976 ↔ Parody 2026 (BOTH eras are satirized).** Neither world is real-world history or real-world forecast. Following the F-is-for-Family pillar, every brand, band, movie, product, and politician in BOTH eras gets a fake parody equivalent. The "future" 2026 isn't a faithful prediction — it's an *exaggerated* present-day, the way 1976 is an exaggerated past. Tech can be better (or weirder) than real 2026; cultural absurdities can be sharper. The world feels recognizable but is clearly *not* the player's world. Symmetry is the law.
- **1976 ↔ 2026 mirror via July 4th (load-bearing).** Mark swaps OUT of 2026 on the eve of America's 250th anniversary (Semiquincentennial). He swaps INTO 1976 on the eve of the Bicentennial. The Act 3 climax is the 1976 July 4th Bicentennial beach concert. The ending happens on or near the 2026 July 4th Semiquincentennial. Exactly 50 years apart. Same holiday. Same scale of celebration. This mirror IS the structural skeleton — it makes the swap feel *inevitable*, not arbitrary.
- **The Tear (machine mechanic, load-bearing).** The first machine failure didn't just burn the device — it *tore something* between 1976 and 2026. A small instability. Future swaps could *use* it (which is how the comms-notebook syncs at all) but every use stretches the tear wider. If Mark and Danny don't swap back and stabilize it from both ends, reality begins leaking — modern objects/sounds/people glitching into 1976 (and vice versa). Player sees the tear escalate visibly across Act 3 — phantom 2026 cars on 1976 streets, PAL's voice cutting through payphones, modern car horns from nowhere. At All-Hope-Is-Lost, the tear is *loud* and others almost notice. This is the cosmic stakes that force Mark home. **Older Danny's "I made improvements over the years" ending line means: he spent decades inventing tear-free swap tech, which is why he's the only one who could pull off the Sylvia rescue.**
- **Mark Cole** = future-kid consciousness (the player). **Danny Marek** = 1976 host consciousness. 1976 cast says "Danny" to the player; 2026 cast says "Mark" to Danny. Never collapse the two names in writing.
- **Danny acted without Mark's consent.** Danny assumed Mark would either sleep through it or be "smart enough" to handle it. Both assumptions were wrong.
- **The machine is broken from the start.** Mark sees it ruined on the table within the first 4 minutes of the game.
- **Comms cadence (Mark ↔ Danny across time):** LIMITED. At most 1–2 communications per act. The mechanism is TBD (will be designed after the arc locks). Specific comms beats Mark has called out:
  - **Act 1 — first comms:** Mark tells Danny "I hate it here, I want to come back." Danny replies "I hate it here too, I want to come back."
  - **End of Act 2 — final comms of that act:** Danny says: *"I'm getting the idea that you don't want to come back."*
- **Plastic-sheet window (Act 2 finale button):** End of Act 2 closes on Mark balancing a chair on the **second-story** windowsill, feet hanging out the (sheeted) window 8-10 feet above the ground, beer in hand, staring into the night. He has fully settled into 1976. **DO NOT spoil or callback before the Act 2 button.** (Note: also update Opening Beat 3 — Mark's first reaction to pulling the sheet should register the second-story drop.)
- **Iwona-heartbeat scene (Act 2):** Mark holds Iwona; it's so quiet he can hear her heart. Direct sonic inversion of the Opening Beat 1 noise. This is the emotional fulcrum of Mark's "I want to stay" turn.
- **July 4th 1976 Bicentennial beach concert (Act 3 climax):** At the Old Bay County equivalent of Ocean City ("Ocean Side" / the boardwalk). Big crazy beach concert with a fake-parody headliner band. Almost certainly: Iwona present, machine arc converges here.
- **Iwona never knows about the swap until the ending — specifically, until she steps out of the Lamborghini.** This is a hard rule. The romance has to land authentically *before* the reveal — meaning Mark has to win her as himself, not as a confession. Implication to flag for sequel material: after Mark swaps back at end of Act 3, Iwona is dating someone she thinks is "Danny" but who is now actually the real Danny. She'll feel something is off ("I knew something was off after that summer" — Older Sylvia's possible ending callback). Park as polish/sequel material; do not resolve in this game.
- **Novel-grade character work for ~10 main-line characters.** The game operates like a nice novel. No throwaway NPCs in the main cast. Every named main-line character has interior life. Sub-characters (parents-of-friends, smoking card-game ladies, Donny the bully, etc.) get one well-drawn trait each and serve as stereotype-flavor / environment. The ~10 main-line slots (subject to revision):
  1. Mark / Danny (two sides of one character)
  2. Iwona Kowalski
  3. Kay Marek
  4. Dennis Marek
  5. Bobby Marek
  6. Mickey Karras
  7. Athena Karras *(or Gus — pick one)*
  8. Peter Kowalski
  9. Maggie (the senile grandma who sees through people)
  10. One TBD wildcard (possibly Natan, possibly someone new)
- **Era Teaching System** — the 70s teaches itself through gameplay, not cut-scenes. See dedicated section below.
- **Emotional arc shape:**
  - **Act 1:** Mark hates 1976, desperately wants out. Danny (in 2026) hates 2026, also desperately wants out.
  - **Act 2:** Mark transitions hate → love (Iwona dance scene is the pivot). Danny does NOT come to love 2026 but stops actively hating it — cracks form in his "get me out" stance. Act 2 ends on the window-and-beer button.
  - **Act 3:** Mark realizes he MUST go back — for three converging reasons (the Tear / Danny's mask-drop / the truth about what Iwona is loving). Finishes the machine. Climax at the Bicentennial concert. Returns to 2026 alone, believing forever.
  - **Ending:** The surprise. Older Danny pulls off the rescue Mark never imagined.

### Story beats in order

**1. The notebook reveal (gameplay starts, ~5 min)**
- Mark opens the notebook past the READ ME letter. Finds: parts list (machine repair manual) + a hand-drawn map labeled *"If something goes wrong — bury this in the lockbox under Buster's headstone, Old Bay Pet Cemetery. I'll find it."*

- Mark goes downstairs and steps out the front door.

**2. The Block Walkout — fish-out-of-water (gameplay, ~3–5 min)**
- **No cut-scene.** Player gets full controls. Camera pans up as the door opens.
- The block IS the tutorial:
  - Bobby's plywood-and-brick bike ramp halfway built in the yard.
  - A lawn dart stuck in the lawn from yesterday.
  - Two kids ride past on banana-seat bikes, no helmets, one steering with no hands holding a slingshot.
  - Across the street: shirtless dad mowing, cigarette in mouth, bare feet, kid trailing with the rake.
  - Driveway: older teenager waxing a muscle car, 8-track blasting parody-rock through open windows.
  - Background sounds: Bombard Mark IV (parody Big Wheel) scraping concrete, screen door slamming, someone yelling for Donny to get inside for dinner.

- **Mark's only VO line during the entire sequence** (subtle, one sentence):
  > *"...where are all the parents?"*
- That's the whole fish-out-of-water beat. *Absence* tells him the era as much as presence. Then he walks up to the first NPC.

**3. The Tutorial NPC ladder — meeting people who already know "Danny"**
- **First NPC: Bobby** (low cover-blow risk). Bobby is the perfect tutorial — small brain, exposition-friendly ("when are you gonna fix my bike? You said yesterday you would!"), annoying-by-design. Establishes the Cover Risk Meter gently: weird Mark behavior just makes Bobby say *"Daaanny you're being weeeeird,"* not raise alarms.
- **Second NPC: Mickey Karras** (higher stakes — peer with deep knowledge of "Danny"). Lives in the Dungeon (garage). First place Mark might find machine parts (electronics, amp gear). Sample tutorial-through-frustration beat:
  > **Mickey:** "Dude, hand me the church key."
  > **Mark:** "...the what?"
  > **Mickey:** "The CHURCH KEY. From the drawer. We do this every day."
  > **Mark:** *(finds bottle opener)* "...isn't a church key—"
  > **Mickey:** "A CHURCH KEY IS A BOTTLE OPENER, MAN."
  > *Player learns: "church key" = bottle opener. Forever remembered.*
- **Third NPC: Kay** (adult — sees through bullshit but is too distracted to care). She's the hardest to fool *in theory* but functionally the safest because she's not looking.
- This is the **difficulty ramp built into the cast** for Act 1.

**4. Parts gathering (gameplay, the bulk of Act 1)**
- Mark uses the notebook's parts list to scavenge Zone 1. Some parts are easy. Some require fooling NPCs. Friends function as quest givers and helpers.
- Parts count for Act 1: **TBD** (pitching 2–3 parts findable in Zone 1 — Mickey's garage, Karras basement, Bobby's stash). Larger parts are saved for Acts 2 (broader town) and 3 (Ocean Side / antique store / harder finds).

### Locked cut-scenes

#### The Jump

**Setup:** Mark feels the neighborhood kids have soured on "Danny." They see him as a wimp (because he isn't actually Danny — he doesn't have Danny's recklessness or vocabulary). He's at desperation.

The kids set up 7 garbage cans across the alley as a bike jump. **No one actually plans to jump it.** One kid already did 4. Seven is a JOKE setup — Mark doesn't realize this. The kids tease him: *"What are you, a sissy fag afraid of falling off a bike?"* This is just how kids talked in 1976. Mark, raised in 2026 anti-bullying culture, hears actual hostility.

Mark says he'll do the jump. The kids are *shocked.* They start to realize Mark thinks the dare is real. *"Dude, we are only fucking with you. We just set up the 7 cans as a joke."* But by then Mark is up the alley on the bike, ready to go.

**The jump:** Mark is scared as he approaches. Hits the last garbage can. Misses the landing ramp. Crashes into the alley fence. Bleeding hand, skinned leg, cut face. Partially crying. Starts yelling at the kids: *"I guess you were right. I'm a loser. Go ahead, let it rip — I'm a TOTAL loser."*

The kids don't hear him. They're running over in **excitement**. *"Dude, you almost had it."* *"You're the king of the neighborhood."* One kid says: *"You only missed because of the wind."* (There was no wind.) The other kids latch onto it: *"Oh yeah, definitely the wind."*

**The awakening:** Mark realizes he's not badly hurt. He feels *stronger than ever*. A kid wheels the bike over — wobbly wheel, but fixable. Mark snaps.

> **Mark:** "Fuck that. Set it up again. I'm doing it."

He grabs the bike. War face. War cry. Pedals back up the alley.

**The second attempt:** **The kids go silent.** The whole alley goes silent. Because now they realize he might actually pull it off and they're holding their breath. Mark hits the ramp. Airborne. Clears all 7 cans. Lands clean.

The kids EXPLODE.

**Why this scene matters:** Mark just discovered something about himself that PAL and the 2026 world had never let him discover. He has agency. He has a body that can do things. He can be hurt and choose to do it again. **This is Mark's pivot from outsider to insider** — and the confidence it gives him is what makes the Iwona dance scene land.

#### Dancing with Iwona

**Setup:** Iwona has been a growing presence through Act 2. Mark has talked to her. She's friendly, eccentric, accented English, hard to read. She's teaching him to dance in her family's basement. Friendly, not seductive. Just two kids doing a thing together — which is how love actually starts.

She's a **trained ballroom dancer** (from Poland). He's a 14-year-old American boy who has never danced. **She is also vulnerable in this scene** — she's used to trained partners; he keeps stepping on her feet; she's trying not to laugh; she's also off-balance trying to make it work with him. Both of them are exposed.

She twirls across the basement. **Lands in his arms** — partly choreography, partly because she lost her balance trying to make a complicated move work with an untrained partner. Her chest presses against his. He's shaking.

Everything around them goes still. The boiler hum stops registering. The clock stops registering. The basement smell stops registering. He realizes how **quiet** it is.

> **Mark:** *(half-whisper)* "It is so quiet here. I can hear your heartbeat."

> **Iwona:** *(whispered, accented — pitched line, Mark to approve or replace)* "...don't tell anyone."

**Scene ends. No first kiss.** The first kiss is reserved for Act 3 at Ocean Side.

**Why this scene matters:** Sonic inversion of the Opening Beat 1 (Mark drowning in 2026 notifications). The thing he's been *missing his entire life* — silence, presence, another human's actual heartbeat — has arrived in the form of a person. This is when "fit in" becomes "love."

#### End of the Day (window/beer)

**Setup:** All the neighborhood kids playing as it gets dark. Camera angle: **overhead shot looking down on a streetlight above the playing kids.** The streetlight turns on. The kids stop. The world stops. They begin to head home.

This was a real 70s thing. Universal cultural marker. Gen X / older players will recognize it instantly. Gen Z will absorb it as alien and beautiful.

Mark is in sync with them. He stops when the light comes on. Heads home. He's still bloody and scabbing over from The Jump earlier in the day.

Before he reaches the Marek house, a neighbor friend tosses him a **can of beer.** Mark **tucks the beer down the front of his pants under his T-shirt** — standard 70s teen-boy beer-smuggling move. Walks past Kay without incident.

**The window setup (PAYOFF for Opening callback):**

Mark goes upstairs to his **second-floor** bedroom. Grabs the old outdoor chair from the closet. Sets it up across the windowsill — chair half-inside, half-outside the open window (the plastic sheet has been pulled aside or removed). His legs hang in the air **8-10 feet above the ground**. Extremely dangerous. He doesn't care.

He opens the beer. Stares peacefully out into the night, drinking.

**Player perspective:** This transition is **playable, not cinematic.** Player walks Mark upstairs, into the room. A brief micro-puzzle to position the chair on the windowsill correctly (drag and place). Then automatic VO + visual takes over.

He looks like a **different person.** Not the same kid who left in the morning. Bloody, scarred, a beer in his hand. He looks no longer a little child.

**The notebook closer (silent visual + voiceover):**

While Mark sits in the chair drinking his beer, the notebook on his desk **glows softly** (the comms window is open). Danny's voice — quiet, almost a sigh — over the visual:

> **Danny:** *"...I'm getting the feeling that you don't want to come back."*

Page goes blank. Glow fades. **ACT 3.**

**Why this scene matters:** The visual is contentment. The subtext is the trap — Mark has built a life he doesn't want to leave, and Danny has noticed. Whatever the All-Hope-Is-Lost moment turns out to be in the final cut, this scene will carry that weight underneath.

#### The Machine Catches Fire (Again) — All Hope Is Lost

**Setup:** Mid-to-late Act 2. Mark and Danny have reassembled what they think is the full machine. The notebook says one test-fire is needed before the real swap-back. Mark powers it on. Player triggers the test.

**The failure — playable, then cinematic:**

The hum builds. Lights flicker. The notebook page glows *wrong* — not its usual warm gold, harsh white. Mark's hands shake on the player-controlled lever.

Then the bedroom **tears.**

For about eight seconds, the walls of Danny's 1976 bedroom go semi-transparent and **2026 bleeds through**: a corner of Mark's PAL-lit bedroom, his own modern desk overlapping Danny's, a single 2026 car horn from a street that isn't there. PAL's voice — calm, smooth, wrong — comes through the open window: *"Mark. Mark? Mark, your heart rate is —"* and cuts off.

A neighbor dog starts barking and won't stop.

The machine catches fire. Real fire, not movie sparks. Mark beats it out with a towel. The fire is small, but the device is *dead* — slumped, blackened, the central crystal cracked clean through. Same way it died the first time.

The tear closes. The walls solidify. The bedroom is just Danny's bedroom again. The dog is still barking.

**Forced inactivity (player gameplay):**

Player is locked to the bedroom. No exit. No interactions outside the room. The notebook will not glow. Danny will not answer. The smoke alarm is dead (1976). Mark sits.

Player explores. Re-examines every object in the room they've walked past for the entire game. New micro-VO triggers — a thing that was a one-liner in Act 1 is now elegiac. The photo of Bobby on the dresser. The Kowalski house through the bedroom window with one light on.

**The clue — buried in the notebook:**

Eventually the player thumbs back through the notebook pages they've already "read." On a page mostly about transistor wiring, in cramped handwriting in the margin, there is a sentence the player almost certainly skimmed past the first time:

> *"if it tears, i can fix it from my side. but only after we swap. you have to come back. don't argue."*

Player realizes Danny *knew this could happen.* Danny planned for it. The fix is on the 2026 side. **But it only works if they swap first.** And they can't swap until the machine is rebuilt at full strength — which means Ocean Side. Which means the parts only available there. Which means Act 3.

**The leap of faith (player commit):**

The player has to *choose* to keep going. Game offers no other path forward. To exit the room and start Act 3, the player must close the notebook and put it under the mattress (a small, deliberate physical action with the controller — like the "swap switch" press in Act 3 will be). The room unlocks.

Mark hasn't fixed anything. He's just decided to trust Danny enough to keep moving.

**Why this scene matters:** This is the puzzle Mark wanted: *"having something the player can do to solve the Dark Night of the Soul problem during gameplay would be the greatest."* The player doesn't solve the machine. The player solves their own willingness to keep going. The Tear is the cosmic stakes. The leap of faith is the emotional one. Both lead the same direction — Ocean Side.

- **Comms 2** — mid-Act, after the basement dance. Danny notices Mark's tone has changed. Prickly: *"You sound weird. You like it there now? Hurry the fuck up."* First hairline crack in his "get me out" stance. He covers fast.

> **Tempo:** Fastest act. ~3–4 hours of play. Opens immediately after Act 2's window/beer button. Closes when Mark pulls the swap switch in the dunes behind the Ocean Side boardwalk on July 4, 1976.

### Beat 1 — The invitation (playable, ~30 min)

Morning after the window/beer scene. Mark wakes up alone, hungover-not-hungover, the notebook still under the mattress. Walks the block. Finds Iwona on the Kowalski porch peeling potatoes (Mary's task, foisted onto her).

The conversation drifts to summer plans. Iwona, casually, in her direct Polish-English way:

> **Iwona:** *"In Poland we had a real beach. The Baltic. Cold but real. Here you have what — this? Linden Drive sprinkler? Pfffft."*
> **Mark:** *"There's Ocean Side."*
> **Iwona:** *"Ocean Side. *(rolls the words like she's testing them)* It is the ocean, yes?"*
> **Mark:** *"Yeah."*
> **Iwona:** *"...show me."*

She doesn't say it like a joke. She says it like a small dare.

Mark, who in 2026 would have nothing to offer, suddenly remembers: the Mareks aren't going to Ocean Side this year. But the Kowalskis *might.* Eight people piled into one rented station wagon, one rented row house on a side street near the boardwalk — peak 1976 Polish-immigrant beach vacation.

Player navigates the social puzzle of getting Iwona's father Jack to invite Mark + one buddy. Possible solves: Mark offers to help pay (1976 cash money from saved comic-buyback quest in Act 2), Mark offers to help carry / babysit Eva, Iwona herself pushes. Plus one of Mark's neighborhood friends — the player chooses which friend to invite *(open: needs locking with Mark)*.

By end of beat: trip is on. Leaving in two days. Mark has to prep.

### Beat 2 — Comms 1 + Tear begins (cinematic + ambient)

Notebook glows that night. Danny is *furious.*

> **Danny:** *"Beach. You're going to the BEACH. The machine is sitting in pieces in OUR bedroom and you're going to the BEACH."*
> **Mark:** *"There's a part I need at Ocean Side. Antique store on the boardwalk. Gilbert U-238."*
> **Danny:** *"...how do you even know what that —"*
> **Mark:** *"I read your notebook."*
> **Danny:** *(long pause)* *"Fine. Get the U-238. Don't get caught with the U-238. Be back by July 5. We're running out of —"* *(page glitches; sentence cuts; word appears, then unappears)* *"...time."*

After comms closes, the bedroom is silent. Then, for one frame, the player sees a **modern silver sedan** parked across Linden Drive where no car should be. Then it's gone. PAL's voice from nowhere: *"Mark, I'm having trouble locati—"* Cut.

**The Tear has started leaking.**

### Beat 3 — Pre-trip prep (playable)

Standard quest beats: pack, get cash, say goodbye to Kay (who hugs him weird-long because *she* doesn't know it's goodbye but the player does), tell Bobby he can play with anything in the closet, slip the notebook into the suitcase.

**Tear escalation beats player will catch (3–4 across this beat):**
- A modern car horn from a street that isn't there
- The Marek kitchen radio briefly plays a song that hasn't been written yet, then snaps back to AM-1976
- Bobby asks, *"Where's PAL?"* — then looks confused at his own question and goes back to coloring

### Beat 4 — Drive to Ocean Side (cinematic montage)

Mark + chosen friend wedged in the back of the Kowalski station wagon with Eva on someone's lap, three kids on the bench, Mary and Jack up front, eight-track playing Polish disco. AM radio fades in and out. Mark looks out the window. Iwona, across the seat from him, catches his eye in the side mirror.

Stops: gas station with a vending machine that takes wrong-decade coins for one frame. A roadside diner. A real beach-bound traffic jam. Eventually: the bridge over the bay, and the boardwalk skyline of Ocean Side rises against the late-afternoon sun.

### Beat 5 — Day 1 Ocean Side (playable)

Walk-the-boardwalk hub. Saltwater taffy. Skee-Ball. The smell of fried clams (Mark VO: *"...what is that smell?"* Iwona: *"That is summer."*). A Tilt-A-Whirl that legally should not be operating.

**The Gilbert U-238 quest (locked exchange):**

The antique toy store *Bayside Curiosities* sits at the far end of the boardwalk. In the display case: a faded yellow-and-red box labeled *Gilbert U-238 Atomic Energy Lab — Now With REAL URANIUM!* (real product, 1950, real uranium ore samples included.)

Earlier comms exchange Mark and Danny had, replayed in player's head as Mark looks at it:

> **Mark:** *"How do we get uranium?"*
> **Danny:** *"In a toy store."*
> **Mark:** *"Be serious."*
> **Danny:** *"...I am being serious."*

Player negotiates with the shopkeeper. The price is wrong-decade-expensive. Player puzzle: barter with a 1976 collectible Mark has from Act 2.

**Iwona's "different" line:**

That evening, on the boardwalk, away from her siblings, leaning on a railing watching the surf:

> **Iwona:** *"You are so different than the other boys. They are loud and stupid. They push and they yell. You are quiet. You think first. You see things. *(she looks at him, not joking)* I don't know how a boy from this neighborhood became like this."*

She means it as the best compliment she knows how to give. Mark hears it as a sentence handed down. **She has fallen for a person who does not exist.** Real Danny will return. Real Danny is not quiet, does not think first, does not see things.

Mark says nothing. Iwona lets the silence be. The first Tear-glitch she *almost* notices — a modern jogger passes under the boardwalk lights in lululemon shorts for half a second — but she's looking at Mark, not the boardwalk.

**End of Day 1.** Mark lies awake on a rented mattress next to the snoring friend. Stares at the ceiling.

### Beat 6 — Comms 2: the mask drops (cinematic)

Late that night. The notebook glows.

> **Danny:** *"Mark."*
> **Mark:** *(waits)*
> **Danny:** *"Mark. I'm scared. I want to come home. Please."*

Four sentences. No jokes. No "fuck." No engineering. The Lou Dorchen mask is on the floor.

Page goes blank. Mark sits with it.

### Beat 7 — Day 2: build (playable, Tear severe)

Mark spends most of Day 2 in the rented row house basement, U-238 in hand, building. The friend covers for him with Mary (off "fishing"). Iwona drifts in and out — bringing him a sandwich, sitting on the basement steps watching, not asking.

**Tear escalation peaks:**
- A 2026 emergency-alert tone blares from the rental's broken transistor radio for two seconds, then dies
- Mark looks out the basement window and sees, in the rental's driveway, *his own 2026 silver sedan*. He blinks. It's a 1976 woody station wagon. He blinks again. Still the wagon. But he saw it.
- Iwona, on the basement steps: *"You hear that? Like a — phone. But not. Did you hear it?"* Mark: *"...no."* She frowns. Lets it go.
- PAL's voice, from inside the basement walls, faint: *"Mark? Mark, are you —"*

The machine is complete by sundown. Tested clean (no fire — the U-238 stabilized the central crystal). Ready.

### Beat 8 — July 4 night: the kiss (cinematic + light input)

Boardwalk. Bicentennial blowout. The crowd is thick. Red-white-blue everything. Cheap flags, cheap hats, cheap beer in cheap cups.

Center stage on the boardwalk's main pavilion: **"Lynyrd Skybird"** (parody-Skynyrd) is closing the show. *(Open: real fake-band name + fake-Freebird song name still TBD with Mark.)* The lead singer has the beard. The guitarist has the hat. The bass player is drunk. The crowd loves them.

Mark, Iwona, and the friend push through the crowd. Eva on Iwona's hip. Iwona hands Eva off to Mary at the back of the crowd and pulls Mark forward — *"come on, come on, I want to see"* — and the crowd swallows them. The friend is lost two rows back. It is just Mark and Iwona.

The band hits the closer's long bridge. **The crowd quiets.** This is the part where the lighters come up. A thousand small flames against the dark. Iwona looks at Mark. Mark looks at Iwona.

> **Iwona:** *"...this is so American."*
> **Mark:** *"It really is."*

She kisses him.

The bridge ends. The guitar solo **EXPLODES.** Fireworks launch off the pier on the downbeat — perfectly timed because the band rehearsed with the fire chief. The crowd loses its mind. Mark and Iwona kiss in a pocket of quiet that has, somehow, formed around them — the noise is the loudest moment of 1976 but they are inside the eye of the storm. The kiss caused the fireworks. The kiss caused the solo. The kiss caused the year.

When it ends, Iwona pulls back, grins, and says nothing.

Mark says: *"I have to find my friend."*

He doesn't.

### Beat 9 — The dunes (playable: the leap)

Mark slips out of the crowd. Walks past the pavilion. Past the last food stand. Past the boardwalk railing. Down into the dunes behind the pier where the dark is real.

The case with the machine is buried under a beach blanket where he stashed it at sunset. He digs it out. Sets it on the sand. Opens it. The central crystal glows.

**Player gameplay:** ONE BUTTON. The swap switch. The screen shows Mark's hand on it. The player must press. Game offers no other path. No timer, no fail-state, no encouragement, no music. Just the hand and the button and the sound of fireworks far away and the surf.

If the player waits long enough, faint over the surf: *"Mark. I'm scared. I want to come home. Please."* Danny's voice from inside the case.

Player presses.

### Beat 10 — Act 3 button (cinematic)

White flash. The sound of fireworks cuts.

Mark opens his eyes.

Ceiling: white. PAL notification chime: soft. Bedroom: his.

**ACT 3 ENDS.**

## ERA TEACHING SYSTEM (design pillar, applies to all acts)

> The 70s teaches itself through gameplay, not cut-scenes. Players who care can dig in; players who don't still absorb the world subconsciously. We respect player time at every layer.

**Layered system:**

| Layer | Method | Player effort | What we teach |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Ambient** | Background NPC animations, environmental detail | Zero | "This world is dangerous and unsupervised" |
| **Diegetic** | Examine objects → Mark VO reacts as a 2026 kid | Optional click | Specific 70s artifacts (cigarettes, payphones, etc) |
| **Conversational** | NPC frustration when Mark fakes 70s knowledge | Forced by quests | 70s vocabulary, social norms (e.g. "church key" = bottle opener) |
| **Collectible** | Scrapbook / lunchbox / Danny's Cool Stuff Drawer | Opt-in completionism | Deep cultural lore, banned toys, era artifacts |
| **Story-critical** | Quest items literally ARE 70s objects (machine parts) | Required | Big-hitter dangerous toys (Gilbert U-238 atomic lab, etc.) |

**Sample examine-VO lines** (Mark reacting to 70s objects):
- **Cigarette vending machine:** *"Wait. A machine. Selling cigarettes. With a pull-knob. Where you put COINS. Where's the ID check? Where's the ID CHECK?"*
- **Pay phone:** *"I don't... I don't have coins. Where am I supposed to get coins. WHY DOES THIS HAVE COINS."*
- **TV with tin foil on rabbit ears:** *"...is this a sculpture? A 1976 sculpture about defeat?"*
- **Metal playground slide in July sun:** *"That's gonna give a kid third-degree burns. That's a CHILD oven. I'm gonna call CPS — wait. There IS no CPS."*

**Inspiration sources:** Disco Elysium (sausage-paragraph examine system), BioShock audio diaries, What Remains of Edith Finch (environment-as-exposition), Red Dead Redemption 2 (background NPCs performing life), Outer Wilds (drop player in, let them figure it out).

**No cut-scenes for any of this** except act-opening cinematics. The 70s teaches itself by being *present* and *interactive*.

**Reference need:** Mark to provide the "70s kid culture" list (lawn darts, deadly metal playgrounds, banned/toxic toys, etc.) — that list seeds both the ambient layer AND the machine-parts quest design.
