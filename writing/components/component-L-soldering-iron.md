## Why this part?

In-fiction, the Book tells Danny he needs *"a controllable heat source for joining small metal contacts. Pen-sized. Plugs into a wall. You'll find one at the hobby store."* The 2026 player thinks **soldering iron**. Danny thinks **wood-burning kit** — because in 1976, that's where a 14-year-old could legally walk in with $10 and walk out with a heating element rated to scorch pine.

**Why this specific object:** Wood-burning kits were a real 1970s phenomenon — sold openly to children as "creative craft tools." The iron itself is basically a soldering iron with a different tip. The kit comes with the iron, a too-hot transformer, scraps of pine to scorch designs into, and almost no safety warnings. Many kids of the era have a small forearm scar from one. The whole bit is that Mark, a 2026 adult mind, is *horrified* that this exists and *thrilled* that it solves his problem.

**The 2026-player "aha":** Danny didn't ask for a "soldering iron" because in his head, that's a grown-up term. He asked for "the thing my friend got for Christmas to burn his name into a board." The Tear repurposes the era's casual attitude toward giving children dangerous things.

## The journey

**Beat 1 — Hook (Book entry):** Morning of Act 1, day after the time-machine inciting incident. The Book glows on the desk. New entry: a hand-drawn sketch of a pen-shaped tool with heat lines, an electrical plug, and Danny's handwriting: *"Hobby store. Eight bucks. Don't tell Mom what it's for. Workbench setup is on you — clear the floor of your closet. You'll figure it out."* Mark VO: *"Eight bucks. Cool. I have... five."*

**Beat 2 — The cash gap:** Mark counts his money. Comes up short (this teaches the money economy in one beat — the player now *needs* the allowance, or a hustle). The Book offers a one-time nudge: *"Check Dennis's car. He drops change."* Player heads out. Finds ~$1.50 in the front seat, $0.75 in the couch. Still short. The player has options:
- **Wait one day for allowance** (Loop 1 from money-economy.md — pacing-equalizer cost)
- **Knock out a mini-earn** (mow a lawn, return bottles to the corner store, check phone booth coin returns)
- **Sell a personal item** (Bobby's Slinky? *No — that's J's quest later. The Book gently steers away.*)

This is the first time the player learns money in this game is **earned, not stolen**, and that the day system is real.

**Beat 3 — The walk to Burnsville:** First Zone 2 trip. Cinematic-light: Mark walks out of the Marek front door, down Linden Drive, the camera shifts to a wider scale, era-music kicks in. He passes Tony's house on Oak Park Road (Tony's shirtless on his lawn chair — *world-building beat, no interaction yet, but the player notices and wonders*). He passes the corner store, the gas station, finally Burnsville Electronics. The doorbell tinkles.

**Beat 4 — The store and the question:** Burnsville interior. First time inside. The `[BURNSVILLE-OWNER]` is behind the counter — older man, reading the paper, doesn't look up. The shelves are dense: model rockets, transistor radios, wire spools, vacuum tubes in dusty boxes, and on a low shelf — the **wood-burning kits** (visible price tag: ~$8.50). Mark grabs one, brings it to the counter. The owner finally looks up.

> **`[BURNSVILLE-OWNER]`:** *"You know how to use this?"*

This is the **first Mark Test moment in the game.** Three responses on the wheel:

- **"Yeah, my dad showed me."** *(Lie — smooth, cover holds, $8.50 transaction completes, owner nods.)*
- **"I'm gonna make a sign for my room."** *(Plausible kid answer — cover holds, owner chuckles: "Don't burn the house down." $8.50 transaction completes.)*
- **"Uh. Yeah. Sort of."** *(Stammer — owner pauses, looks at him longer than is comfortable. Cover takes a small ding. Transaction still completes — but the Book later notes: "He'll remember you." This becomes a small future flag in `tear-system.md` instrumentation.)*

No option to back out. Player committed when they grabbed the box. This teaches that **choices are real and the cover is real.**

**Beat 5 — Install (the workbench reveal):** Back at Mark's bedroom. He locks the door (or wedges a chair under the knob — Kay-proof, not Bobby-proof). He pulls open his closet. The Book entry updates with a quick diagram: where the bench goes, where the iron's transformer sits, how the cord routes so it can't be seen from the doorway. Mark clears the floor, sets the kit on a piece of cardboard, plugs in the transformer. The transformer **hums and gets warm** (era-correct — these things ran hot enough to scorch carpet). The iron tip glows red. The room smells like hot metal and burnt plastic.

Mark VO: *"Okay. Workbench exists."* The closet floor is now the workbench. The room is now Mission Control. The machine spine is **real**. End of quest L.

## Mark-from-2026 VO beats

The unique recoil/recognition moments this quest produces:

1. **The wood-burning kit reveal:** *"This is... they sold this to kids. They sold this *to kids.*"* — The recoil. Adult Mark recognizing a normalized hazard.

## Era-teaching surface

What the 2026 player learns about 1976 by doing this quest:

- **Wood-burning kits existed and were marketed to children.** Hot enough to burn skin, sold without parental safety briefing.
- **Hobby stores were a real thing.** Not Amazon. You walked there. You looked at shelves. You picked things up.
- **Kid money was small but heavy.** $8 felt like real money. A purchase had weight.
- **Cashiers asked questions.** Strangers in your neighborhood knew you, or noticed you, or remembered you. Anonymity wasn't the default.
- **Dennis's car had loose change.** Cars were change-collectors. Parents didn't track it. Kids knew it.
- **Closets became forts/labs.** Bedrooms were full kid territory in a way they're not now. The door locked. Parents didn't routinely enter.

## Open questions / risks

1. **Cash cost — is $8.50 the right number?** Anchored to money-economy.md (~$80–100 total game need, ~10% feels right). Verify when we wire `quests.md`.
2. **Real brand or generic kit?** Real-life examples: ATF Wood Burning Set, American Toy & Furniture wood-burner. We've already gone parody for amps (BOMBARD). My lean: **generic** — the kit's box just says "WOOD-BURNING SET" in big yellow letters. Save the parody-naming bandwidth for things that recur (beer, band names).
3. **Does the stammer-path "he'll remember you" flag matter later?** Lean **yes, faintly** — when Mark returns to Burnsville for component B (glass-blowing set, much bigger purchase), the owner can squint at him: *"You again."* That's a tiny cover-tax that pays off the small choice. Worth implementing.
4. **Should Kay almost-catch Mark coming home with the bag?** Could be a 30-second hide-the-bag-in-the-bushes beat that teaches stealth-around-mom mechanic. **Lean yes,** but very light — one prompt, one choice, no fail state.
5. **Does Tony being on his lawn during the walk happen *every* walk, or just first time?** Lean: **first-time only as a foreground beat,** then ambient/background on subsequent walks. Tony becomes a "set piece" the player stops noticing — until Q-IDEA-04 promotes and he's suddenly *relevant*.
6. **What happens if the player tries to buy the kit before having enough money?** Owner says *"Come back when you've got eight-fifty, kid."* No shame, no fail. Just a soft bounce. Player exits and figures it out.
