## Why this part?

In-fiction, the Book tells Danny he needs ferrous spring steel wound in a uniform helix. The 2026 player goes *"...a Slinky?"* and that's the joke. The most iconic dumb toy of the era happens to be precision-engineered induction coil material. The Tear's machine needs *exactly* what a four-year-old whined for in 1968.

**Why this specific object:** Slinkies were *everywhere* in 1976. Every kid had one or had broken one. They were the first toy you "ruined" — once a Slinky got kinked, it never recovered, and every kid in America had at least one ruined Slinky in their closet. The whole bit is that Mark goes looking for a *pristine* one and discovers that's actually rare.

**The 2026-player "aha":** Danny didn't reach for an exotic part. He reached for the toy he was supposed to play with. The Tear repurposes childhood.

## The journey

**Beat 1 — Hook (Book entry):** Morning after installing L. The Book glows. New entry: a sketch of a coiled spring with arrows showing induction, a list of properties (length, gauge, ferrous), and Danny's handwriting: *"You'll know it when you see it."* Mark VO: *"Oh, you have GOT to be kidding me."*

**Beat 2 — The wrong Slinkies first:** Mark goes to his own closet. Finds Bobby's Slinky — kinked, knotted, half the coils stuck together. Useless. Tries to straighten one coil at the workbench (L). Book rejects: *"Uniform helix. Try again."*

**Beat 3 — The negotiation round:** Mark has to find a kid on the block with an intact Slinky and *trade* for it. Two or three doors to knock on, each with a different kid's currency:
- `[KID-WITH-SLINKY-OPTION-1]` wants baseball cards (TRADE leverage if Mark has them).
- `[KID-WITH-SLINKY-OPTION-2]` wants a favor (small EARN — sneak them a candy cigarette from the corner store).
- `[KID-WITH-SLINKY-OPTION-3]` won't trade for anything because their grandma gave it to them — closed door, lesson about *what* a kid will and won't part with.

**Beat 4 — The friction:** One kid offers to *sell* it. *"My brother said this is worth fifty cents at the flea market."* (It isn't — Mark from 2026 knows it's worth nothing.) The player can pay (cash drain) or hold out and try another door. **Crucially, the "steal it" option is never on the wheel.** If the player asks the Book "can I just take it?" Mark VO says *"No. I'm not that kid."*

**Beat 5 — Install scene:** Back at the workbench. Mark uncoils the Slinky with a hand-cranked jig (CRAFT). It springs back. He clamps it. He winds it onto the core former. The machine emits a single, low *hum* — the first sound it has ever made. Bobby walks in. *"What's that noise?"* Mark slams a blanket over the rig.

## Era-teaching surface

What the 2026 player learns about 1976 by doing this quest:

- Slinkies were *everywhere* and also *fragile* — the universal "first toy you ruined" experience.
- Kid trade economy was real currency. Cards, candy cigs, favors. Not money first.
- Some toys were sentimental — kid-scale heirlooms. Not everything had a price.
- Doors on the block were knockable. Kids ran their own diplomacy without parents involved.
