# 1970s Kid Valuables — The Black-Market Economy

> **Source:** Mark's dump, 2026-05-23.
> **Purpose:** Define the **currency layer** of the game. Machine parts cost relationships AND stuff. This is the "stuff." Feeds `gameplay-loop.md` (TRADE mechanic) and `quests.md` (every NPC trade puzzle).
> **Tone rule:** This is the *contraband* economy — what kids actually wanted, what they would actually trade for. Some of it is illegal in 1976 too. The comedy is that ten-year-olds had this stuff in their sock drawers.

---

## The economy in one paragraph

Mark needs machine parts. Some parts cost cash (corner store), some cost relationships (a war-vet's basement, the older kid's stash), but **most cost VALUABLES** — the underground tradeable goods of a 1970s American kid. This is a barter economy, not a money economy. Kids in 1976 had *almost no money* but they had *piles of stuff* — and they traded that stuff with elaborate folk-rules about what was worth what. We model that.

**Mark's starting inventory:** essentially nothing. Danny had some stuff (the closet) but Mark doesn't know what's there yet. The first time the player rummages through Danny's room, they should find a *few* starter valuables. Then they scavenge, trade up, climb the food chain.

## The valuables tier list

### S-tier — the highest currency

#### 1. Playboy Magazines
- **What it is:** Real product, ubiquitous in 1976. Found in Dad's closet, the construction site dumpster, the back of the older kid's bike basket.
- **In-game role:** **TOP-TIER CURRENCY.** A single Playboy is a unit of currency that gets you nearly anything from a kid 12+. The cover-girl name on the spine matters (some issues worth more — collector logic kid-style).
- **Acquisition:** RARE find in construction-site dumpster (the woods behind the Andersons), or one of the older kids will *sell* you one for big money. NEVER ask a parent or shopkeep.
- **Risk:** Kay catches you with one → grounded for the day → lost scavenge time. Big risk, big reward.
- **Tone discipline:** We never show the content. The magazine is a *prop*. The joke is that **kids treated it like a currency without really understanding what it was.** Mark VO sees it for what it actually is. Bobby treats it as a treasure map of a country he hasn't been to.
- **Comedy beat:** Mark finds one and Mark VO does the Disco Elysium examine: *"Miss July is a person. Miss July is forty-eight years old in 2026. Miss July is somebody's grandma."*

#### 2. Beer (full can, unopened)
- **What it is:** A full can of cheap American 1976 beer — National Bohemian, Schaefer, PBR, Schlitz, Old Milwaukee. (We can pick a parody-brand name for the dominant local one — "Old Bay Lager" suggests itself.)
- **In-game role:** **MAJOR CURRENCY** among older kids. Worth less to the 8-12 set, who can't open them without help.
- **Acquisition:** Grab from Dennis's basement fridge (RISKY — Kay counts), out of someone's garage, swiped from a neighborhood cookout, or won at the older-kid card game. Already used as a story prop in Act 2 End-of-Day scene.
- **Risk:** Getting caught carrying a beer = parent intervention = grounded.
- **Comedy beat:** Mark, who in 2026 doesn't drink because nobody his age drinks anymore, learns that in 1976 a beer in your waistband is a *passport*.

#### 3. Cigarettes (full pack or even loose)
- **What it is:** Marlboro, Winston, Kool, Camel. Or parody brand. Pack of 20.
- **In-game role:** **MAJOR CURRENCY** — *plus* a social tool. Offering a cigarette to an older kid changes how he talks to you.
- **Acquisition:** **THE VENDING MACHINE** at the bowling alley, the gas station, or the laundromat. Anyone could feed quarters in. No ID. This is one of the all-time most-shocking-to-Gen-Z era teaching moments. Also: steal a pack from Dennis's carton on the fridge (RISKY — counted). Or trade up.
- **Risk:** Kay smells them. Bobby tells. Iwona disapproves quietly.
- **Comedy beat:** Mark VO at the vending machine: *"It's a vending machine. For cigarettes. In a bowling alley. Where children are. ...wait, *I'm* a child."*
- **Cross-reference:** *Era teaching surface — the cigarette vending machine is one of the great visual gags we have. Should appear in at least 3 locations.*

#### 4. Pot (Marijuana)
- **What it is:** A small baggie. Always borrowed from a much-older sibling, never directly purchased by our 14-year-old.
- **In-game role:** **S-TIER CURRENCY among the oldest kids only** (16+). For Mark/Danny's age (14) this is not something they use — it's something *worth a lot* to the older kids whose attention they sometimes need.
- **Acquisition:** Very limited. Single source. Probably **an older neighborhood kid** (NPC TBD when a quest needs him) who deals to his friends. Player can scavenge one stash from his hiding spot if they figure out where it is. ONCE.
- **Risk:** Catastrophic if found by any parent. End-of-game-flag-level grounded.
- **Tone discipline:** The bit is **the kids treat it like trading cards.** Mark VO is the only sensible voice. Never glamorized — it's just *there* in 1976 the way energy drinks are there in 2026.
- **Comedy beat:** Mark trades a baggie to the older kid for the *Austin Magic Pistol* (calcium carbide for Component G). Mark VO: *"I just traded a felony for a Geiger-counter component."*

### A-tier — the workhorse currency

#### 5. Fireworks (M-80s, bottle rockets, jumping jacks, Roman candles)
- **What it is:** Illegal-in-Maryland fireworks smuggled up from South Carolina or Pennsylvania by an older brother. M-80s are the king — quarter-stick equivalent, banned 1966 but easily found in 1976.
- **In-game role:** **A-TIER CURRENCY** — and **PART-feedstock** (silver nitrate / black powder for Component G). Dual-purpose.
- **Acquisition:** Trade up from the older neighborhood kid. Or scavenge from his garage stash if you can get in. Or July-4-eve fire-sale at the older brother's pickup truck.
- **Risk:** Fingers. Eyes. The bowling alley sign.
- **Comedy beat:** Bobby finds one in Mark's pocket. *"What's THIS!"* — chase scene around the house.

#### 6. Arcade Tokens
- **What it is:** Brass tokens from the local arcade (the bowling alley back room, the pizza place, the boardwalk). Sometimes branded with the arcade's name; sometimes generic. Worth 25¢ each but only spendable AT that arcade.
- **In-game role:** **A-TIER** — universally desired by every kid because *every kid wants more time on the cabinet*. Also small-PART potential — brass tokens for some electrical contact in the machine (if we need a brass component).
- **Acquisition:** Earn at the arcade itself (high-score bonus, manager's good mood). Find on the ground at the bowling alley. Trade for them. Win them in marbles.
- **Comedy beat:** Mark, who has never touched a coin-operated anything, looks at an Asteroids cabinet like an alien artifact. Then gets the high score because he's a 2026 kid and the game is *trivial* to him.

#### 7. Movie Theater Passes
- **What it is:** Real paper passes — comp tickets the theater manager handed out to local businesses for promotions. Mark's actual childhood memory: *"we would find some left out on the manager's desk at our movie theater."* PERFECT in-game.
- **In-game role:** **A-TIER** — a stack of free movies is a hot commodity. Plus the *act of getting them* is a stealth/social mini-quest.
- **Acquisition:** **HEIST QUEST.** The theater manager's office. Player has to time the popcorn rush, slip in, grab a small stack (not too many — he'll notice). Could be one of the most fun set-pieces in the game.
- **Comedy beat:** Mark looking at the marquee — fake-1976 movie titles we get to invent. Per the F-is-for-Family pillar: "Super Star Adventures" / "Smokin' Trucker" / "JAWZ" (the parody-Jaws is too easy to skip).

#### 8. Chrome Caps (bicycle tire valve caps)
- **What it is:** Chrome bike-valve dust caps, often **stolen off the tire valve stems of parked semi-trucks at the truck stop.** Every kid wanted these on their Schwinn. Specific to Mark's neighborhood culture, beautifully niche.
- **In-game role:** **A-TIER cosmetic currency.** Kids judge each other's bikes by these. Has secondary use in machine: chrome caps are good electrical contacts.
- **Acquisition:** **HIGH-RISK SCAVENGE QUEST.** Bike to the truck stop. Wait for a parked truck. Unscrew the cap. Don't get caught by the trucker. (Truckers in 1976 would *chase* you.)
- **Comedy beat:** Mark VO crouched behind a Peterbilt's wheel well: *"I am stealing from a man who can lift his own house. ...for a piece of metal that weighs half a gram."*

### B-tier — the working currency

#### 9. Boombox Batteries (D-cells)
- **What it is:** 4-6 D-cells per boombox. Eveready or Duracell. Expensive in 1976 (~$1-2 each, real money to a kid).
- **In-game role:** **B-TIER** — universal because *every older kid's boombox dies constantly.* Also **PART** candidate — D-cells are obvious workbench power source.
- **Acquisition:** Drugstore (cash). Scavenge from anyone's *dead* battery pile (don't tell them you took them). Trade.
- **Comedy beat:** Mark, who has never bought a battery, gets sticker shock at the drugstore. Drugstore clerk: *"They go FAST, kid."*

#### 10. Camera Film Rolls (Kodak 110 / 126 / Polaroid SX-70 packs)
- **What it is:** Unused rolls/packs of film. Photography wasn't free in 1976 — you paid per shot AND for developing.
- **In-game role:** **B-TIER niche** — only the kid with a working camera wants these. BUT: film is required for **the Scrapbook collectible system** (`gameplay-loop.md` era-teaching surface #4) if we let Mark take in-game Polaroids.
- **Acquisition:** Drugstore. Trade.
- **Comedy beat:** Less of a comedy beat, more of a *quiet* beat — Mark using a Polaroid for the first time, watching it develop. Iwona watching with him. The kind of moment Gen Z will pause on.

### C-tier — common but useful

These are also valuables but at lower exchange rates. We probably don't fully spec each one — they fill the trade table.

| Item | Notes |
|---|---|
| **Comic books** | Mint condition matters. *Amazing Spider-Man* + *Iron Fist* peaking '76. Real currency among the 8-12 set. |
| **Baseball cards** | Topps. Star players (Reggie Jackson, Johnny Bench, Pete Rose). Mickey collects. Trade puzzle. |
| **Marbles** | Tournament currency. Cat's-eyes, steelies, shooter. Mark can WIN these in playground tournaments. |
| **Bottle caps** | Beer bottle caps are quietly hoarded by a certain kind of kid. 5¢ glass-Coke-bottle deposits also work as cash. |
| **Beer cans (empty, collectible)** | Pull-tab cans of obscure regional beers. Hipster-before-hipster. A specific-NPC currency when we eventually surface that kid. |
| **Wacky Packages / Topps stickers** | Trading-sticker craze 1973-76. Real. |
| **Gum cards / Topps non-sport cards** | *Wacky Packages, Planet of the Apes, Star Wars cards (1977 — just barely past us)* |

## NPC currency preferences (first pass)

The trade game gets interesting because **different NPCs want different things.** This makes the economy non-fungible — you can't just stockpile one valuable.

| NPC | Wants most | Won't accept | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Mickey** | Cigarettes, fireworks, baseball cards | Anything "babyish" (Shrinky Dinks) | Hard-edge teen energy |
| **Bobby** | Wacky Packages, marbles, Shrinky Dinks | Nothing — he loves everything | Bobby's a soft trade |
| **Eva (Iwona's sister)** | Shrinky Dinks, dolls, jewelry trinkets | "Boy stuff" | Hard negotiator for Easy-Bake access |
| **Iwona** | Doesn't trade. Gives. | — | Romance arc, not commerce |
| **The theater manager** | Doesn't trade. Targeted for heist. | — | Adversary, not partner |

*(NPCs above are people already locked in `people.yaml`. New NPCs — the dealer-kid, the war-vet basement guy, the beer-can collector kid, etc. — will be added when a specific quest needs them, not before.)*
