## SECTION 1 — The Freedom Doctrine (zero supervision as lifestyle)

This is the single most foreign concept the player will encounter. Gen X players nostalgically remember. Gen Z players literally cannot conceive of it. The game lives or dies on whether we sell it.

### The pillars

- **Disappear for 10–12 hours.** Out the door at 9 AM, back by streetlight. No way to be contacted. Not neglect — *normal.* The default. A parent who *did* keep tabs on you was the weird one.
- **No helmets, no pads, no problem.** Bikes, skateboards, minibikes, sleds, anything with wheels — full speed, no gear. Helmet laws didn't exist for bikes until much later.
- **Be home by dark.** The first streetlight is the universal "stop" signal. The world *quiets*. Everyone drifts home. Already locked as a **gameplay mechanic** in `gameplay-loop.md` (day rhythm).
- **Porch yelling = parental contact.** No cell phones. Moms stand on the porch and SHOUT the kid's name. Up and down the block. Other moms hear and pass the call along: *"MARK COLE, YOUR MOM IS CALLING YOU"* relayed three houses over. Already on Mark's list. THIS IS A MECHANIC: when Mark is mid-quest and Kay's call goes out, the player hears it from across the map.
- **Roaming miles from home on bikes.** No permission. No check-in. The block, the next block, the creek, the woods, the dump, the construction site, the strip mall a mile away.
- **Latchkey at 7-10.** Kids as young as 7 letting themselves in, making their own snacks, watching themselves until parents got home from work.
- **Resilience > protection.** Injury was *expected.* Parents' default reaction to a scrape was "rub some dirt on it" or "walk it off." The Jump scene in Act 2 already encodes this — the kids reframe a crash as a triumph because in 1976 *trying counted more than landing.*
- **Trial-and-error learning.** Safety lessons came AFTER something went wrong. The hot stove. The wasp nest. The chain that came off the bike at 25mph downhill.
- **Conflict resolved without adult mediation.** Playground politics were a republic of small people. Disputes settled with rules invented mid-argument, with "do-overs," with the rare actual fight, with elaborate honor systems.

### How this lands in gameplay

- **Mark cannot call home.** Period. There is no phone in his pocket. PAL is gone. The notebook is to Danny, not to Mom. The player feels the *absence* of the safety net immediately.
- **NPCs do not check on the player.** The player feels *trusted* to a degree that may be alarming for Gen Z.
- **The streetlight day-clock** is the only *enforced* parental rule.
- **NPC reactions to injury are wrong (right).** Mark crashes the bike. Kay glances over. *"Did you wash your hands?"* Continues making dinner. This is the bit.

## SECTION 2 — The Playground & Street Hazards

### Playgrounds were endurance tests, not play spaces
- **Metal slides** — June sun heated to 140°F+. Burns. Yes really.
- **Concrete surfaces under everything.** Asphalt under monkey bars. No rubber, no mulch, no soft fall zones.
- **Splintered wood** — splinters were *constant.* Every kid's mom owned tweezers and a needle "for splinters."
- **Rusted everything.** The merry-go-round axle. The teeter-totter pivot. Tetanus boosters were a regular pediatric event.
- **Witch's Hat / spinning umbrella.** Centrifugal-force ejection. Yes really.
- **Tall single-pivot see-saws** with no center safety stop. The one with the rope.
- **Old-generation steel toys** — castles with pointy parts (Mark's note). Tin-soldier sets with sharp tin. Cap guns with metal hammers.

### Street-sport rules (and how they actually worked)
- **"Tag, you're it"** with no boundaries — you tagged across yards, into the alley, through the old guy's hedge at the end of the block.
- **Kickball, with bases drawn in chalk on a STREET that had CARS on it.** "Car!" called out → game pauses → car passes → resumes.
- **"Two-hand touch" football** in the same street.
- **"Pickle"** between two bases, runner caught in the middle — universal game, no equipment needed.
- **Hide-and-seek "Olly Olly Oxen Free"** — sundown cue.
- **Wiffle ball** in someone's yard — bases were trees and shrubs.

### The "kid law" system
- **Do-overs** were a real currency. You could call one. They could be denied.
- **"I didn't see it."** legitimate ruling.
- **The oldest kid was the judge** unless someone older showed up.
- **A fight could end a friendship for a day** and then resume.

## SECTION 3 — Bikes & Mobility

This deserves its own section because **the bike is the player's primary verb** (locked in `gameplay-loop.md` core loop).

- **Schwinn Sting-Ray** with banana seat + sissy bar — the iconic 1976 kid bike. Mark rides one (presumably Danny's).
- **Banana seats** were designed for *two* — kids regularly doubled up.
- **Card-in-the-spokes** — clothespin a baseball card to the fork so it ticked the spokes and sounded like an engine. *Real Mark memory category.*
- **Chrome valve caps** — see `kid-valuables.md`. Currency layer.
- **Bike playing-card customization.** Pegs, streamers, mirrors.
- **Reflectors and chain guards mandatory by code; nobody enforced.**
- **Bike jumps from plywood + cinder blocks** — already canon (The Jump scene, Act 2).
- **Older brother's 10-speed** = aspirational tier. Some kids had hand-me-down adult 10-speeds way too big for them.
- **Truck beds** — Mark's note. Kids rode in pickup truck beds, no question, no law. We probably need a *scene* of this — possibly the ride to Ocean Side in a truck bed if not in the Kowalski wagon. Beautiful Gen Z "wait WHAT" moment.

## SECTION 4 — Media: TV, Radio, Music, Phones

### Television (THE central living-room artifact)
- **A few channels only.** ~5 broadcast — the three networks (ABC, CBS, NBC), one PBS, one local independent. UHF if you were lucky.
- **Rabbit ears + tinfoil** for reception. Real Mark memory. Every set had aluminum foil on the antenna tips. THIS IS A GAMEPLAY MOMENT — Mark fiddling with the antenna to get the news in is a micro-puzzle.
- **Rotary dial channel selector.** Big satisfying CLUNK.
- **Color TV was relatively new** — many households still had B&W in 1976.
- **Cable TV was nascent and rare.** The wired remote with a turn dial is real — Mark's link confirms.
- **The TV went OFF at night** — playing the national anthem and then static. Real.
- **Boxy wood-grain cabinets.** Furniture, not screens.
- **Saturday morning cartoons** were *the* week's event. ABC, CBS, NBC each had their own block. *Schoolhouse Rock.* *Super Friends.* *Land of the Lost.*
- **The TV Guide** was a household object. Knew what was on. No streaming, no menu.

### Radio
- **AM and FM, both alive.** AM = news, sports, talk, oldies-already. FM = rock, album-oriented.
- **DJ shoutouts and dedications.** Mark's note: *"You could call into radio stations once a week and leave dedications to girls you had a crush on."* CONFIRMED, peak 1970s tradition. POSSIBLE GAMEPLAY: Mark calls in a dedication to Iwona. Plays on the kitchen radio. Iwona's mom Mary hears it. Knowing/suspicious look across the porch.
- **The DJ knew you.** Local DJs were minor celebrities.
- **8-Tracks** — already in story-arc (Polish disco in the Kowalski wagon). Big, clunky, *changed tracks mid-song* with a CHUNK.
- **Cassettes** — newer, smaller, you could *record off the radio*. Real Mark-era teenage skill: finger on the REC + PLAY buttons, waiting for the song.
- **45 and 33 vinyl records** — Mark's note. The 45 spindle adapter ("spider") is a perfect *examine-an-object* gag.
- **Record players** — large, fragile, needle had to be set down by hand. Bobby will inevitably skip a record.
- **Boomboxes** — older big-radio variant of what would later be a portable. The transistor was already there; the loudness wasn't yet. Battery currency layer (see kid-valuables).

### Phones
- **Rotary phones in homes.** Heavy, satisfying mechanical click of each digit. Wall-mounted in the kitchen. Coiled cord stretched across the room.
- **Phone booths everywhere.** Real Mark research: each had its own phone number. You could call a *specific phone booth* and someone might pick up. Kids made elaborate games of this.
- **Long-distance cost money.** "Don't call long distance without asking" was a parental rule. Mark's link confirms.
- **411** = directory assistance, free in 1976.
- **0** = operator, for collect calls, busted phones, emergencies before 911 was universal.
- **Time and weather numbers** — ME 7-1212, GR 2-1212. Automated. Mark's note is dead-on. PERFECT examine-the-payphone gag.
- **Two-letter exchange names** — MEridian, GRover. Real. Phasing out by 1976 but still printed on older signs and matchbooks.
- **NO 911 in many areas yet.** 911 rollout was 1968-onward but spotty through the 70s.
- **Party lines** still existed in some neighborhoods — multiple households sharing a line. Pick up and someone else is talking.

### Movies
- **Marquees, ticket booths with a person inside.** Hand-stamped tickets.
- **Drive-in theaters** were still very alive. Cars full of kids in pajamas, speakers hooked over the window.
- **Theatre passes** — see kid-valuables, S-tier.
- **Movies in 1976** (parody options for our marquee): *Rocky, Taxi Driver, Carrie, All the President's Men, Network, The Omen.* Plus second-run summer reruns of *Jaws* (1975). We rename them all per F-is-for-Family pillar — *"JAWZ"*, *"Roxy"*, *"The Spawn"*, etc.

## SECTION 5 — Money, Stores, Commerce

- **No ATMs.** People got cash by going to the bank during banker's hours (M-F 9-3) or cashing a check **at the grocery store**. Real.
- **Credit cards existed but rare** — Visa/Master Charge. **Carbon-paper imprint machines** at the register. Real Mark research. Store kept a paper book of stolen card numbers to check against.
- **Layaway** was huge. Big-ticket items put aside, paid for in installments, picked up months later.
- **Stamps in the grocery store** — S&H Green Stamps. Real, peak in 70s. Collect in books, redeem for toaster.
- **The corner store** (or 7-Eleven, opened 1927 already widespread by 76) — penny candy, Slurpees, comic-book spinner rack, cigarette vending machine in the corner, magazine rack with the Playboy on the top shelf.
- **The drugstore** — film, batteries, candy, Polaroid film, school supplies, the pharmacist who knew your mom by name.
- **The hardware store** — wood, nails, paint, gas cans you filled yourself, fishing tackle, ammo (no ID).
- **The dime store / five-and-dime** — Woolworth's, Kresge's, Murphy's. Cheap toys, candy by the pound, a lunch counter inside that served burgers.

## SECTION 6 — Cars, Driving, Travel

- **No bucket seats — bench seats.** Front and back. *"Like a couch."* Three across the front.
- **No seat belts worn.** They existed in the cars after 1968 federal mandate but **nobody used them.** Kids sat on parents' laps. Babies were held.
- **"Pumpkin seats"** — early car seats, real name. Plastic shell, lap belt, not crash-tested in any meaningful way. Doubled as house chairs and feeding chairs. Mark's note is correct.
- **Children riding in pickup truck beds.** Already noted.
- **Smoking in the car with windows up.** Universal. Kids in the back seat marinating in it.
- **Drinking and driving** — socially accepted in a way it isn't now. Open container in the cupholder, beer at the lake then drive home.
- **The drive-thru bank teller** with the pneumatic tube.
- **AAA Triptik** — paper folding strip maps for road trips. No GPS, no phones. Pre-trip planning at the kitchen table.
- **CB radio in the car.** See banned-toys N2 + machine parts. Convoy culture.

## SECTION 7 — Smoking, Drinking, Substance Norms

- **Smoking EVERYWHERE.** Restaurants, planes, hospitals, doctor's offices, the **children's wing of the hospital**, the elementary school teachers' lounge.
- **Cigarette vending machines anywhere.** No ID enforcement.
- **Drinking age was 18 in most states** including Maryland in 1976 (raised to 21 federally by 1984). Drinking-age inconsistencies between neighboring states fueled cross-border teen runs.
- **Smoking on TV ads ENDED in 1971** (per the 1970 Public Health Cigarette Smoking Act) — by 1976 cigarette ads were out of TV but **still everywhere in magazines, billboards, sponsorships.**
- **Joe Camel didn't exist yet** (1987) — but the Marlboro Man was peak iconic.

## SECTION 8 — Home Life

### Kitchen
- **Push mower with no engine.** Real Mark memory. Reel mower. You pushed, it spun, it cut. Lawns took forever.
- **Steel lunchboxes with matching Thermos.** *Real currency among kids — your lunchbox said who you were.* Star Wars boxes (1977 — just past our window, we use *Super Star Adventures* or whatever). *Land of the Lost. Charlie's Angels. The Bionic Woman.* The Thermos was glass-lined inside; if dropped, the glass shattered inside and you'd find shards in your soup.
- **Avocado-green appliances. Harvest-gold.** Real.
- **Tab. Fresca. Tang. SpaghettiOs. Wonder Bread.** The pantry of a 1976 kitchen.
- **The microwave was new and viewed with suspicion.** 1976 was early adoption. Kay might or might not have one.

### Yard / Garage
- **The Garage was Dad's domain.** Workbench, peg-board with tool silhouettes, transistor radio playing the Orioles game.
- **Siphoning gas with your mouth** to start the lawn mower or fill the go-kart. Real. Mark's note about building go-karts from lawn-mower engines + shopping cart wheels + plywood = REAL ESTABLISHED CULTURE. THIS COULD BE A QUEST.
- **Burning leaves in the yard** in fall. Smoke smell of October.
- **The Slip 'n Slide** on the lawn. The garden hose for everything else (drinking included, per Mark's note).

### Bathroom Medicine Cabinet
- **Mercury thermometer.** See banned-toys N3.
- **Mercurochrome / Merthiolate** — red-orange antiseptic painted on every cut. Contained ethyl mercury. Banned by FDA in 1998.
- **Hot water bottle.** Heating pad alternative.
- **"Take an aspirin and lie down"** = universal parental medical advice.

## SECTION 9 — School / Daily Life

- **No backpacks on wheels.** Books carried in stacks under one arm.
- **The dittograph / mimeograph.** Purple ink. Slightly toxic. Kids sniffed it.
- **Cursive penmanship enforced.**
- **Dodgeball in PE** — peak. Eliminated by safety culture much later.
- **Recess was unsupervised** at the perimeter.
- **The lunch lady** — institutional figure.
- **The chalkboard and the eraser** — clapping erasers as punishment / privilege.
- **Patrol kids with orange sashes** at crosswalks — child supervising child crossing the street.
