# Banned & Toxic Toys — 1970s Source Material

> **Source:** Mark's dump, 2026-05-23. Verified + annotated + structured by Clawpilot.
> **Purpose:** This file feeds **two** downstream docs:
> 1. `machine-rules.md` — the parts list (every machine part is a toy/object from this catalog)
> 2. `quests.md` — the per-toy acquisition mini-quest, NPC involvement, comedy beat
> 3. (bonus) `era-research/kid-culture-70s.md` — broader cultural texture
> **Rule we're following:** parody-1976 leeway IS in play, but realer = funnier. We default to real history and only fictionalize when the parody bit demands it. Where we change something for parody, we flag it [PARODY: …].

---

## Verification legend

- ✅ **REAL** — existed in/before 1976, broadly as Mark described.
- 🔶 **REAL but later** — real product, but recall/peak danger postdates 1976; we keep it because parody-1976 absorbs minor anachronism.
- ⚠️ **REAL with caveat** — Mark's framing slightly off; correction noted.
- 🎭 **FICTIONALIZED** — real type of product / parody-permissible invention. We name it ourselves.

## In-game role legend

- **PART** — feeds the machine directly (becomes a Component).
- **TRADE** — currency between kids. Used to barter for PARTS we can't scavenge.
- **HAZARD** — pure comedy / world-texture / health-tax beat. Not a part. Not for trade. Just *there.*
- **LORE** — collectible / scrapbook / TV-ad / era-teaching surface. Background atmosphere.
- *(Many items wear two hats — e.g. Cap Gun is HAZARD when fired at Mark + PART when stripped for the impact-sensitive compound. We dual-tag.)*

## Target Act legend

- **A1** = Act 1 (block walk, intro to hazards)
- **A2** = Act 2 (rebuild machine in Old Bay — most parts land here)
- **A3** = Act 3 (Ocean Side antique store + the U-238 capstone)
- **ANY** = ambient texture, can appear any time

## The catalog — modern toys (Mark's primary list)

### 1. Clackers
- **Verification:** ✅ REAL. Peaked 1968–71, formally banned in some states by 1976; broken-shrapnel injuries widely reported. Knockoffs continued well into the late 70s on the black-kid-market (older brother's closet).
- **In-game role:** **HAZARD + TRADE.** Mickey carries a pair. Possible **PART** dual-use: the acrylic balls can be cracked to harvest the inner weight as a small pendulum / counterweight for Component F (gyroscope stabilizer).
- **Comedic angle:** Mark VO on first encounter: *"Two heavy plastic balls on a string. Designed for an eight-year-old. Banned in nine states. Mickey has nine sets."*
- **Quest beat:** Mickey challenges Mark to a Clacker contest. Player loses → bonk on the wrist, day-end VO complaint. Player wins → earns Mickey rep + the cracked pair as a part.
- **Target act:** A2

### 2. Lawn Darts (Jarts)
- **Verification:** ✅ REAL. Sold throughout the 70s. CPSC didn't ban until **1988** — but the metal-tip "weighted dart you throw in an arc at children playing in the next yard" was peak 1976.
- **In-game role:** **HAZARD + PART.** The metal tips are forged steel — perfect for Component C (the grounding spike). One Jart yields one tip. Set comes with four.
- **Comedic angle:** Dennis Marek owns a set in the garage. Mark VO: *"Imagine if a Frisbee could give you a tetanus shot."* Bobby asks if he can play. Dialog choice: *"NO."* / *"...fine, but stand THERE."*
- **Quest beat:** Find Dennis's set. Sneak one out without him noticing it's missing (he plays Sunday tournaments). Block reputation cost if Dennis catches you.
- **Target act:** A2

### 3. Creepy Crawlers / Thingmaker
- **Verification:** ✅ REAL. Mattel, 1964-onward, sold heavily through the 70s. The Plastigoop + 390°F open metal hotplate combo is exactly as described. Burns + fumes both real.
- **In-game role:** **PART (the hotplate itself).** The Thingmaker's heating element is the heat source for the workbench crafting station. Plastigoop is also a craft ingredient for the machine's insulating cells.
- **Comedic angle:** Mark VO: *"It's a 390-degree open hotplate. They sell it next to the dolls."* Kay shouts upstairs about a *smell* during the first crafting attempt.
- **Quest beat:** Find Danny's old Thingmaker in the basement. Functional. Plastigoop nearly empty — quest sub-objective to find more (drugstore, or trade with Mickey).
- **Target act:** A2 (Component build-out)

### 4. Baby Alive (Kenner, 1973)
- **Verification:** ✅ REAL. Kenner's Baby Alive, 1973-onward. Mechanical mouth, packets of dehydrated baby food + water, the doll "chewed" and excreted into a diaper. Real reports of the mouth-gear catching hair and fingers; required tool to disassemble for cleaning. Genuinely unsettling.
- **In-game role:** **HAZARD + LORE + TRADE.** Eva (Iwona's little sister) owns one. Bobby is terrified of it. Player can examine. The grinding gears are also a candidate small-PART (Component K's micro-cam).
- **Comedic angle:** Mark, watching it eat a packet of "green beans": *"That's a real mechanism in there. That's real torque. That diaper is going to be... real."* Bobby refuses to be in the same room.
- **Quest beat:** Eva will not lend it. Trade-puzzle if we need it as a part.
- **Target act:** A2

> **Note 2026-05-23 (per Mark):** This entry replaces the previously-listed *Snacktime Cabbage Patch Doll*, which was a 1996 product and broke our era window. Baby Alive is real-1973, does the same finger-eating joke for real, and keeps us in lane.

### 5. Toy Guns (general — no orange safety tip)
- **Verification:** ✅ REAL. Federal orange-tip rule wasn't until 1988. Hard plastic projectiles, choking-hazard discs, all real.
- **In-game role:** **HAZARD + TRADE.** Block kids have these. Mark gets hit in the head with one in an early Act 1 hazard beat.
- **Comedic angle:** Mark VO on first toy-gun-stickup-by-a-six-year-old: *"In 2026 this would be a 911 call."*

### 5a. Cap Guns (potassium chlorate caps)
- **Verification:** ✅ REAL. Mark's chemistry breakdown is correct. Potassium chlorate / perchlorate + sulfur + antimony sulfide is documented cap composition. Impact-sensitive pyrotechnic. Real.
- **In-game role:** **PART.** This is one of our better parts. Cap-gun caps are the **igniter compound** for Component G (the firing primer). Player has to collect ~20 rolls of caps and harvest the strips.
- **Comedic angle:** Mark VO on the chemistry: *"I'm carrying impact-sensitive pyrotechnic compound in my pocket. ...In a Star Wars lunchbox."*
- **Quest beat:** Buy at corner store, but limit per kid per day. Have to do the run multiple days. OR trade for a stash from an older neighborhood kid who's stockpiling for July 4.
- **Target act:** A2

### 5b. Disc-o-Punch (disk launcher gun)
- **Verification:** ⚠️ REAL but typically called **"Mattel Disc-Shooter"** or **"Whamo Air Blaster"** — "Disc-o-Punch" not finding as canonical. Real category, fuzzy brand. We can [PARODY: name it whatever].
- **In-game role:** **HAZARD + PART.** The launching mechanism — coiled spring + plastic barrel — is the **launch tube** for Component E (the projectile-sync resonator).
- **Comedic angle:** *"Marketed for ages 6 and up. Fires a plastic disc at the velocity of a small bird hitting a windshield."*

### 6. BB-Guns
- **Verification:** ✅ REAL. Daisy Red Ryder is THE iconic 1976 BB gun (*A Christmas Story* would later canonize it). "You'll shoot your eye out" was already a parental refrain in 1976.
- **In-game role:** **HAZARD + LORE.** Probably NOT a machine part — too iconic to dilute. Lives in the world: the kid two blocks over has one, takes potshots at squirrels, eventually at Mark's bike tire.
- **Comedic angle:** Mark gets a BB in the butt. Visible welt for the rest of the day. Kay applies *meat tenderizer* to it, which is an actual 1970s home remedy. Mark VO: *"WHY IS SHE PUTTING SEASONING ON MY BUTT."*
- **Target act:** A1 (intro to hazards)

### 7. LEGO (heavy-metal era)
- **Verification:** ⚠️ REAL but more nuanced. **Cadmium-pigmented red and yellow LEGO bricks: 1960s through ~early 1980s.** Lead was less of a LEGO concern; cadmium was the real one. By 1976 it's still present in the older bricks in Danny's bucket.
- **In-game role:** **PART (small) + TRADE.** LEGO is a small-but-real part (yellow brick = Component J's housing). Also a trade currency among the younger kids on the block.
- **Comedic angle:** Mark sucks on the corner of a yellow brick out of habit (he's stressed). Mark VO: *"...cadmium. I just had cadmium for a snack."*

### 8. Easy-Bake Oven (100W lightbulb era)
- **Verification:** ✅ REAL. The 100W incandescent bulb design ran from 1963 until safer redesigns in the early 2000s. 350°F interior temperatures, real burn hazard. Real.
- **In-game role:** **PART.** The Easy-Bake's 100W socket is the **secondary heat source** for the workbench (Thingmaker is primary). Also yields the **reflective metal liner** as a part.
- **Comedic angle:** It's a **girls'** toy in 1976 — gendered marketing was loud. Mark has to *negotiate with Iwona's little sister Eva to borrow it.* Eva is a hard negotiator.
- **Quest beat:** Eva quest: trade her something + agree to play a tea-party round. Comedy gold.
- **Target act:** A2

### 9. Lemon Twist
- **Verification:** ✅ REAL. Plastic ankle-loop with a weighted lemon-shaped ball. 1970s playground / sidewalk classic. Tripping hazard, broken ankles, exactly as described.
- **In-game role:** **HAZARD + PART (the weight).** The molded plastic lemon contains a small lead-weighted core — that's Component F's swing pendulum if Clackers don't pan out.
- **Comedic angle:** The neighborhood girls are *amazing* at this. Mark tries it. Eats pavement. Hop count: 0.
- **Target act:** A1 / A2 ambient

### 10. Spaceship projectile toys
- **Verification:** ✅ REAL. Mattel "Major Matt Mason," various rocket-launch toys. Hard plastic projectiles that *did* fly distance.
- **In-game role:** **PART (the launching coil) + LORE.** Major Matt Mason figurines are also collectibles for the scrapbook.
- **Comedic angle:** Mark accidentally launches one and it bonks Kay in the back of the head from across the room.

### 11. Toy Fireworks (Snap Pops, Bang Snaps)
- **Verification:** ✅ REAL. Throw-down poppers, silver nitrate + sand wrapped in tissue. Sold over-the-counter in 1976.
- **In-game role:** **PART (silver nitrate trace) + TRADE.** Bag of snap pops is also a common kid-currency around July 4.
- **Comedic angle:** Bobby throws one at Mark's feet. Mark VO: *"...he just attacked me with a chemistry reaction."*
- **Target act:** A2 → A3 (peak around July 4)

### 13. Dip-a-Flower / Whimsy Dip / Dippity Glass
- **Verification:** ✅ REAL. Polyester resin + harsh solvents. 1960s-70s peak. Sharp wire frame. All as Mark described.
- **In-game role:** **PART (the resin).** The polyester resin is a **structural casting compound** for Component H (the lens housing). Excellent.

### 14. Aurora Monster Scenes / Torture-rack model kits
- **Verification:** ✅ REAL and **darker than people remember.** Aurora's "Monster Scenes" line (1971–74) included a literal **"Pendulum"** and a **"Hanging Cage"** as accessories sold to children. Public outcry in 1972 helped kill the line — but unsold inventory was still on shelves through 1976.
- **In-game role:** **LORE + TRADE.** Discontinued = collectible by '76. Kid down the block has a complete set. He'll trade.
- **Comedic angle:** Mark finds the Pendulum kit in Danny's closet. Mark VO: *"Aurora Plastics. Officially licensed. ...by who??"*
- **Target act:** A1 (lore beat) / A2 (trade currency)

### 15. Slip 'n Slide
- **Verification:** ✅ REAL. Wham-O, 1961-onward. Compression fractures, esp. in older kids/adults, are documented.
- **In-game role:** **HAZARD (set-piece scene).** Probably an Act 2 set-piece — neighborhood kids set one up on the hottest day. Mark, who is in a 14-year-old's body but a 14-year-old's *2026* sense of personal-injury awareness, tries it. Cinematic crash.
- **Comedic angle:** Same beat as The Jump in Act 2, but lower stakes — just bruising.
- **Target act:** A2

### 16. Swing Wing / Wing Ding
- **Verification:** ✅ REAL. Wham-O's Swing Wing, 1965-onward. Propeller hat you swung your head in a circle to spin. Neck strain, headaches, concussions all real.
- **In-game role:** **HAZARD + PART (the bearing).** The hub of the Swing Wing is a small smooth-running bearing — Component K (rotational coupler).
- **Comedic angle:** Bobby has one. Demonstrates. Mark VO: *"He's going to need a chiropractor by Thursday."*
- **Target act:** A1 / A2

### 17. Shrinky Dinks
- **Verification:** ✅ REAL. Introduced 1973. Baked in the family oven. Burns from impatient kids reaching in.
- **In-game role:** **TRADE + LORE.** Probably not a machine part — but a great low-stakes activity scene with Bobby or Iwona's siblings. Also: trade currency on the block.
- **Comedic angle:** Mark accidentally makes a perfect Shrinky Dink. Eva is so impressed she pays him for it. *In gum cards.*

### 18. Mattel Slime (boron / mineral oil era)
- **Verification:** ✅ REAL. Mattel Slime, 1976 launch (!) — peak era. Early formulation contained guar gum + sodium tetraborate (borax). Mineral-oil scares came later. Sold in a green plastic trash can.
- **In-game role:** **PART (the borax-bound polymer).** Slime is a **lubricant + insulator** for the workbench. Component D's gasket compound.
- **Comedic angle:** Mark VO: *"It launched THIS YEAR. The 'toy of the year' is a chemistry experiment in a trash can. ...okay, fair."*
- **Target act:** A2 (it's literally a 1976 product — perfect)

### 19. Chemistry Sets
- **Verification:** ✅ REAL. Both brands legitimate.
  - **Porter Chemcraft "1001 Experiments"** — yes, real. Open-flame alcohol burners, real glassware, real reagents. Some 1950s editions did include uranium-bearing samples.
  - **Skilcraft Chemistry Sets** — real, Skilcraft was a major late-60s/70s brand. (Spelling: **Skilcraft**, not Skillcraft.)
- **In-game role:** **PART (the works — flask, burner, reagents) + LORE.** A complete chemistry set in Danny's closet provides multiple Components plus the workbench's lab-glass tier.
- **Comedic angle:** Mark VO opening the case: *"Sodium hydroxide. Sulfuric acid. Mercury. ...For age eight and up."*
- **Quest beat:** Find Danny's set (intact-ish). One bottle missing — quest to find or substitute the missing reagent.
- **Target act:** A2

### 20. Flammable Halloween costumes
- **Verification:** ✅ REAL. Flammable Fabrics Act of 1953 existed but enforcement on children's costumes was minimal pre-1980s. Plastic-and-rayon costumes near jack-o-lantern candles caused regular ER trips.
- **In-game role:** **LORE / HAZARD ambient.** Probably not part-relevant (we don't have a Halloween beat — story is summer 1976). But: PARENT recollection beat in dialog.
- **Comedic angle:** Kay reminisces. *"Remember when little Tommy Carlucci's Spider-Man caught fire? His mom slapped it out so fast."* Mark stares.

### 21. Flammable plush toys
- **Verification:** ✅ REAL. Same root cause — pre-FR-treated fabrics.
- **In-game role:** **LORE.** Bobby's stuffed dog is one. Player can examine.

### 22. Gilbert Glass Blowing Set (1950s)
- **Verification:** ✅ REAL. A.C. Gilbert sold these. Alcohol torch + glass rods + a child. Pre-1976 product, exactly the kind of thing you'd find at a yard sale or in someone's grandparents' basement in 1976. Perfect Act 2/3 antique acquisition.
- **In-game role:** **PART.** Provides custom **borosilicate glass tubing** for Component B (the vacuum chamber). And the alcohol torch is a secondary heat source.
- **Comedic angle:** Mark VO: *"...they sold an alcohol torch to a child. As a 'set.' With a logo."*
- **Quest beat:** Yard sale or basement-of-the-Andersons (or whoever — see open NPCs). Negotiation puzzle.
- **Target act:** A2 (basement find) or A3 (antique store)

### 23. Slingshots
- **Verification:** ✅ REAL. Wrist-rocket era (1970s peak). Surgical-tubing rocket slingshots could put out a window or an eye.
- **In-game role:** **HAZARD + TRADE.** Older neighborhood kid's currency. Possible **PART** — the surgical tubing is the elastic membrane for Component I.
- **Comedic angle:** Mark VO: *"In 2026, the school sends you a letter for HAVING this. In 1976, the school *gives them out as PRIZES.*"* (Slight exaggeration. Not by much.)

### 24. Swing Bikes (a.k.a. Spider Bike / wiggle-bike)
- **Verification:** ⚠️ REAL with caveat. **Swing Bike** specifically was the Lear-Siegler 1976 patent — articulated frame, two-section bike that pivoted at the middle. Real, marketed exactly in our era. Very dangerous, niche.
- **In-game role:** **HAZARD + LORE.** Probably one of the neighborhood kids has one and demos it. Mark tries. Eats pavement. Same beat as Lemon Twist.
- **Comedic angle:** *"It bends in the middle. ON PURPOSE."*
- **Target act:** A2

### 25. Super Elastic Bubble Plastic
- **Verification:** ✅ REAL. Wham-O, late 60s into 70s. Polyvinyl acetate dissolved in acetone, in a squeezable tube, blown through a straw. Acetone fumes, exactly as described.
- **In-game role:** **PART (the acetone-PVA compound).** The membrane material for one of the lens components.
- **Comedic angle:** Mark blows a bubble. Mark VO: *"...I can taste my fillings."*

### 26. Wood Burning Kits
- **Verification:** ✅ REAL. Various 1970s brands (Walnut Hollow, etc.). Soldering-iron-temperature pen given to a 10-year-old. Real.

### A2. Austin Magic Pistol (late 1940s)
- **Verification:** ✅ REAL. Calcium-carbide-and-spit fireball pistol. Children's toy. Real product. Insane.
- **In-game role:** **PART.** This is a **capstone basement find** — the calcium carbide reagent is critical for Component G's ignition train. Also the pistol body itself is a beautiful prop.
- **Comedic angle:** Mark finds it in someone's basement. Mark VO reading the box: *"FUN FOR THE WHOLE FAMILY. ...the fun is fire."*
- **Quest beat:** Basement of an old neighbor — the war-vet grandpa who lives at the end of the block. Player has to listen to his stories. (Era teaching gold.)
- **Target act:** A2 late, or A3 antique store

### A3. Gilbert U-238 Atomic Energy Lab (1950)
- **Verification:** ✅ REAL. A.C. Gilbert, 1950–1951. Contained four real uranium ore samples (autunite, torbernite, uraninite, carnotite), a small polonium-210 alpha source, a Geiger counter, an electroscope, a Wilson cloud chamber, and the *"Prospecting for Uranium"* booklet co-published with the U.S. Atomic Energy Commission. Retail price $49.50 in 1950 ≈ ~$600 today. Famously the most dangerous toy ever sold.
- **In-game role:** **PART — THE CAPSTONE.** Component A (the central crystal stabilizer). Without it the machine cannot complete. This is the *Ocean Side antique store* quest — Mark's stated locked moment.
- **Comedic angle:** Locked exchange already in story-arc.draft.md:
  > **Mark:** *"How do we get uranium?"*
  > **Danny:** *"In a toy store."*
  > **Mark:** *"Be serious."*
  > **Danny:** *"...I am being serious."*
- **Target act:** A3 (Ocean Side antique store — *Bayside Curiosities*)

## Items Mark didn't list that we should add (Clawpilot's nominations)

These are real 1976-or-prior products that fit so perfectly we should consider them. Mark approves/rejects each.

### N1. Daisy Powerline 880 / Crosman 760 air rifle
- Heavier-than-BB pellet rifles. Real. In every garage. Even more dangerous than the BB guns above.
- **Role:** HAZARD escalation. Optional.

### N2. CB Radio (Cobra 29 / Realistic TRC-447)
- Citizens Band radio peaked **EXACTLY 1976** — *Convoy* was the #1 song. Every dad and trucker had one.
- **Role:** **PART** — the CB's quartz crystal + transmitter coil are the most obvious **comms relay** the Tear could be exploiting. This is *very* good. Also a TEACHING beat — kids of '76 spoke in CB slang ("breaker 1-9, good buddy").

### N3. Mercury thermometer / mercury in general
- The Marek bathroom medicine cabinet has one. Bobby drops it. Mark VO knows what mercury is. Bobby treats the silver bead as a *prize.*
- **Role:** **PART (small mercury reservoir for switching component) + HAZARD.** Era-teaching beat #1.

### N4. Polaroid SX-70
- 1972 product, peak in 1976. The chemical packet in the photo — if Mark cuts it open — contains caustic alkaline jelly. Kids did.
- **Role:** **TRADE + LORE.** Probably not a part. But the *capability to take photos in this era* matters for the scrapbook system.

### N5. Schwinn Sting-Ray / banana-seat bike
- The block has a few. Iconic 1976 kid bike. Mark's daily ride is one (presumably Danny's).
- **Role:** **MOBILITY (core).** Not a hazard, not a part — the bike IS the scavenge-loop's main verb. Worth a full callout in `gameplay-loop.md` someday.

### N6. Estes model rocket kits
- Solid-fuel hobby rockets, sold to children, fired from backyards. 1958-onward, huge in 70s.
- **Role:** **PART (the igniter + solid fuel charge) + HAZARD set-piece.** A neighborhood launch goes wrong.

### N7. Wham-O Air Blaster
- Squeeze-bulb air cannon. Blew out birthday candles from across the room. Or your sister's hair. Or a glass figurine off a shelf.
- **Role:** **TRADE + minor PART.**

### N8. Slinky (metal)
- Not dangerous per se, but the metal Slinky is a **PART** candidate — continuous helical-spring conductor, exactly the kind of thing a teen genius would use as an inductor coil.
- **Role:** **PART.** Possibly Component J's primary coil.

### N9. Etch A Sketch
- Aluminum-powder display. Innocent on the outside, glass + heavy-metal powder on the inside. Kids who broke them got a lap full of conductive aluminum.
- **Role:** **PART (the aluminum powder = magnetic-display medium) + LORE.**

### N10. CO2 cartridges / Crosman Powerlets
- Sold in any sporting goods or hobby store. 12-gram CO2. The kid who pierced one with a nail in his garage existed in every neighborhood.
- **Role:** **PART (pressurized gas source) + HAZARD.**
